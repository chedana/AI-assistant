# Harassment and Quiet Enjoyment

## Overview

Every tenant in England has a right to "quiet enjoyment" of their rented home. This is both a common law right and one reinforced by statute, principally the Protection from Eviction Act 1977. Landlords who harass tenants, enter without permission, interfere with utilities, or attempt to force tenants out face criminal prosecution, civil damages, and injunctions.

## The Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

### Common Law Origin

The covenant of quiet enjoyment is one of the oldest principles in landlord-tenant law. It is implied into every tenancy by common law, regardless of whether the tenancy agreement mentions it. "Quiet" in this context does not mean absence of noise — it means **undisturbed** or **without interference**.

### What It Means

The covenant guarantees that the tenant can occupy and use the property without **substantial interference** from the landlord. This includes:

- Freedom from physical intrusion by the landlord.
- Freedom from acts that render the property materially less fit for the purpose for which it was let.
- Freedom from harassment, intimidation, or threats.
- Freedom from interference with utilities or services.

### Implied by Statute

Section 11 of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 implies the repairing obligation. The covenant of quiet enjoyment is separate and is implied by common law into all leases and tenancies. Additionally, under s.3 of the Protection from Eviction Act 1977, it is unlawful for any person to deprive a residential occupier of their occupation without due process.

### Breach Examples

The following are established breaches of the covenant of quiet enjoyment:

- Entering the property without consent or notice.
- Carrying out building works that make the property uninhabitable (e.g., removing the roof, scaffolding blocking windows).
- Disconnecting utilities.
- Persistent unannounced visits.
- Removing the tenant's belongings.
- Changing locks while the tenant is out.
- Threatening behaviour toward the tenant.
- Excessive noise or disruption from works on adjacent property owned by the landlord.

## Protection from Eviction Act 1977

### Section 1 — Unlawful Eviction and Harassment

Section 1 of the Protection from Eviction Act 1977 creates two criminal offences:

### Unlawful Deprivation of Occupation — s.1(2)

It is an offence for any person to **unlawfully deprive** a residential occupier of their occupation of the premises, or to attempt to do so, unless the person proves they believed (and had reasonable cause to believe) that the residential occupier had ceased to reside in the premises.

**Maximum penalty:** Imprisonment of up to 2 years and/or an unlimited fine.

### Harassment — s.1(3) and s.1(3A)

It is an offence to do acts likely to interfere with the peace or comfort of a residential occupier or members of their household, or to persistently withdraw or withhold services reasonably required for occupation, with the intent of causing the occupier to give up occupation or to refrain from exercising any right or remedy.

- **s.1(3):** Acts done with **intent** to cause the occupier to give up occupation.
- **s.1(3A):** Acts which the perpetrator **knows or has reasonable cause to believe** are likely to cause the occupier to give up occupation (this lower threshold was added by the Housing Act 1988, s.29).

**Maximum penalty:** Imprisonment of up to 2 years and/or an unlimited fine.

### Who Can Be Prosecuted

The offences apply to "any person," not just the landlord. This includes:

- The landlord personally.
- The landlord's agent or managing agent.
- A family member acting on the landlord's behalf.
- Builders or contractors instructed by the landlord.
- Anyone else acting to deprive the tenant of occupation.

## Landlord Access Rules

### The General Rule: No Right to Enter

A landlord does **not** have a general right to enter the tenant's home. The property is the tenant's home for the duration of the tenancy, and the tenant controls access. Even if the tenancy agreement says the landlord can enter "at any time" or "with 24 hours' notice," such clauses do not override the tenant's common law and statutory rights.

### When the Landlord May Enter

The landlord may enter the property in the following circumstances:

### With Tenant's Consent

The landlord can enter if the tenant gives permission. Permission should be specific (not a blanket clause in the tenancy agreement), and the tenant can withdraw it at any time.

### For Repairs and Inspections — s.11(6) Landlord & Tenant Act 1985

The landlord has a right to enter the property to view its condition and state of repair, **provided that:**

- At least **24 hours' written notice** is given to the tenant.
- Entry is at a **reasonable time** (typically daytime during working hours, not early morning or late evening).
- The purpose is genuinely to inspect or carry out repairs.

This is the only statutory right of entry, and it is limited to repair-related access.

### In an Emergency

In a genuine emergency (e.g., gas leak, burst pipe, fire), the landlord can enter without notice to prevent harm to the property or people. This is a common law right arising from necessity. However, the emergency must be genuine — the landlord cannot manufacture an "emergency" to gain access.

### To Carry Out Gas Safety Checks

The Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998 require annual inspections. The landlord should arrange these with the tenant's cooperation, giving reasonable notice.

### By Court Order

If the tenant refuses access for legitimate purposes (e.g., gas safety inspections), the landlord can apply to the court for an injunction requiring the tenant to permit access.

## Specific Forms of Harassment

### Changing Locks

If a landlord changes the locks while the tenant is out (or present), this is an act of **illegal eviction** under s.1(2) of the Protection from Eviction Act 1977, unless the landlord has obtained a court order for possession. This is a criminal offence.

**Tenant's remedy:** Call the police (it is a criminal offence). Contact the council's Tenancy Relations Officer. Apply to the county court for an emergency injunction to be re-admitted. Claim damages under s.27-28 of the Housing Act 1988.

### Disconnecting Utilities

Deliberately cutting off or interfering with gas, electricity, water, or other services (internet, shared heating) constitutes harassment under s.1(3) of the Protection from Eviction Act 1977 if done to force the tenant to leave.

Even where a utility is in the landlord's name and the landlord stops paying the bills, this can constitute withdrawal of services under the 1977 Act.

### Removing Tenant's Belongings

Removing, dumping, or destroying the tenant's possessions is:

- A breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment.
- Potentially an offence under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977.
- A tort of trespass to goods, giving rise to a civil claim for damages.
- Potentially criminal damage under the Criminal Damage Act 1971.

If the tenant has left belongings after vacating, the landlord should follow the **Torts (Interference with Goods) Act 1977** — giving the tenant written notice and a reasonable period to collect their belongings before disposing of them.

### Unannounced Visits

Repeated visits without notice, or visits at unreasonable times, constitute harassment if they are intended to, or have the effect of, disturbing the tenant's peaceful occupation. Even where the landlord has a genuine reason (e.g., to show the property to prospective tenants), proper notice must be given and the tenant's consent obtained.

### Threats and Intimidation

Verbal threats, threatening letters, aggressive behaviour, or threats to evict without a court order all constitute harassment. This can also amount to an offence under the Protection from Harassment Act 1997 (which provides for both criminal prosecution and civil remedies including injunctions and damages).

### Interference with Mail

Intercepting, opening, or redirecting the tenant's mail is a criminal offence under the Postal Services Act 2000, s.84.

### Entering Common Areas

In properties with shared areas (e.g., HMOs), the landlord may have greater rights to access common areas (hallways, kitchens in shared houses) but still cannot enter the tenant's private room without consent or proper notice.

## Remedies for Tenants

### Criminal Prosecution

- Report harassment or illegal eviction to the **police** (it is a criminal offence under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977).
- Report to the **local council's Tenancy Relations Officer** — councils have a duty to investigate and can prosecute.
- The **Crown Prosecution Service (CPS)** can also prosecute in serious cases.

### Civil Damages — Housing Act 1988, ss.27-28

If a landlord unlawfully evicts or harasses a tenant, the tenant can claim **civil damages** under ss.27-28 of the Housing Act 1988. The measure of damages is the **difference in value** of the landlord's interest in the property with and without a sitting tenant. In areas with high property values, this can be very substantial — sometimes exceeding £10,000 or even £50,000.

### Injunction

The tenant can apply to the **county court** for an **injunction** requiring the landlord to:

- Allow the tenant back into the property.
- Restore utilities.
- Stop harassment.
- Refrain from specific conduct.

An **emergency (without notice) injunction** can be obtained the same day in urgent cases. The tenant should attend the court with evidence (e.g., photos of changed locks, text messages, witness statements).

### Damages for Breach of Quiet Enjoyment

Separate from the statutory damages above, a tenant can claim damages at common law for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment. These can include:

- **General damages** for distress, inconvenience, and loss of enjoyment of the property.
- **Special damages** for actual financial losses (e.g., hotel costs, cost of replacing belongings, medical expenses).
- **Aggravated damages** where the landlord's conduct was particularly outrageous.
- **Exemplary damages** in exceptional cases to punish the landlord and deter others.

### Rent Repayment Orders

If the landlord commits an offence under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977, the tenant can apply to the **First-tier Tribunal** for a rent repayment order of up to **12 months' rent** (Housing Act 2004, ss.73-74; Housing and Planning Act 2016).

### Protection from Harassment Act 1997

Where the landlord's conduct amounts to a "course of conduct" (two or more occasions) that causes alarm or distress, the tenant can bring a claim under the Protection from Harassment Act 1997. Remedies include:

- An injunction (restraining order).
- Damages (including for anxiety).
- Criminal prosecution (harassment is a criminal offence under this Act).

## Practical Steps for Tenants

### Documenting Harassment

- Keep a **written log** of every incident: date, time, what happened, who was present.
- Save **all communications**: text messages, emails, WhatsApp messages, letters.
- Take **photos and videos** of any changes (changed locks, removed belongings, disconnected utilities).
- Get **witness statements** from neighbours, visitors, or other tenants.
- Keep records of **police reports** and council complaints (reference numbers).

### Immediate Steps if Illegally Evicted

1. **Call the police** — illegal eviction is a criminal offence. Ask for a crime reference number.
2. **Contact the council's Tenancy Relations Officer** — they can intervene and mediate.
3. **Apply for an emergency injunction** at the county court — you can do this without notifying the landlord first.
4. **Contact Shelter's helpline** (0808 800 4444) for immediate advice.
5. **Do not break back in** — this could lead to allegations against you. Use the legal process.

### Reporting to the Council

Every local council in England has a duty to investigate complaints of harassment and illegal eviction. The council's Tenancy Relations Officer or Environmental Health team will:

- Contact the landlord and explain their legal obligations.
- Mediate between landlord and tenant.
- In serious cases, prosecute the landlord.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- You have an absolute right to quiet enjoyment of your home — your landlord cannot enter without your permission (except in genuine emergencies or with 24 hours' written notice for repairs).
- Changing locks, cutting off utilities, removing belongings, and threats are all potentially criminal offences.
- If you are illegally evicted, call the police immediately and contact your local council.
- You can claim substantial damages for harassment and illegal eviction.
- Document everything — a detailed record is your strongest evidence.
- Seek advice from Shelter, Citizens Advice, or a housing solicitor.
