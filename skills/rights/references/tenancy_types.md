# Tenancy Types in England

## Overview

Understanding the type of tenancy you have is fundamental to knowing your rights and obligations. The vast majority of private tenancies in England are Assured Shorthold Tenancies (ASTs), but other types exist with different levels of security. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 will eventually convert all ASTs into assured tenancies, significantly strengthening tenant protections.

## Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST)

### What Is an AST?

The Assured Shorthold Tenancy is the **default** form of private residential tenancy in England for tenancies created on or after 28 February 1997. Under the Housing Act 1988 (as amended by the Housing Act 1996), any tenancy that meets the assured tenancy criteria is automatically an AST unless the landlord serves a notice stating it is an assured tenancy.

### Criteria for an AST

A tenancy is an AST if:

- The tenant is an **individual** (not a company).
- The property is the tenant's **only or principal home**.
- The tenancy is not specifically excluded (see exclusions below).
- The annual rent is more than £250 (£1,000 in London) and less than £100,000.
- The landlord does not live in the same building (unless it is a purpose-built block of flats).

### Key Features

- **Limited security of tenure:** The landlord can recover possession using Section 21 (no-fault) or Section 8 (specific grounds) of the Housing Act 1988.
- **Deposit protection:** The landlord must protect any deposit in a government-approved scheme (Housing Act 2004, s.213).
- **Repairing obligations:** The landlord must maintain the structure and installations (Landlord & Tenant Act 1985, s.11).
- **Tenant Fees Act 2019:** Prohibits most letting fees.
- **Fair rent:** There is no rent cap (unlike regulated tenancies), but the tenant can challenge unreasonable increases via the First-tier Tribunal.

### Exclusions from AST Status

The following cannot be ASTs:

- Tenancies where the annual rent exceeds £100,000.
- Tenancies where the annual rent is less than £250 (£1,000 in London).
- Business tenancies.
- Tenancies granted by resident landlords (where the landlord lives in the same building, unless it is a purpose-built block).
- Holiday lets.
- Agricultural tenancies.
- Student lettings by specified educational institutions.

## Assured Tenancies

### What Is an Assured Tenancy?

An assured tenancy provides **greater security** than an AST. The landlord cannot use Section 21 (no-fault eviction) and can only recover possession using Section 8 grounds. Assured tenancies were the standard form before the Housing Act 1996 made ASTs the default.

### How They Arise

- The landlord serves a notice before the tenancy begins stating it is an assured tenancy (not an AST).
- Housing association tenancies granted before 15 January 1989 may be assured.
- Under the RRA 2025, all ASTs will eventually be converted to assured tenancies (not yet in force).

### Key Features

- **Greater security of tenure:** No Section 21 eviction.
- **Succession rights:** A spouse or civil partner can succeed to the tenancy on the tenant's death (Housing Act 1988, s.17).
- **Rent increases:** Subject to Section 13 procedure.
- **Grounds for possession:** Only Section 8 grounds (mandatory and discretionary).

## Regulated Tenancies (Pre-1989)

### What Is a Regulated Tenancy?

Regulated tenancies are those granted **before 15 January 1989** (when the Housing Act 1988 came into force). They are governed by the Rent Act 1977 and provide the highest level of security for private tenants.

### Key Features

- **Very strong security of tenure:** The landlord can only recover possession on specific grounds, many of which are discretionary.
- **Fair rent:** The rent is registered by the Valuation Office Agency and cannot exceed the registered "fair rent." Fair rents are typically significantly below market rent.
- **Succession rights:** A spouse, civil partner, or family member living with the tenant may succeed to the tenancy (potentially as an assured tenancy for non-spouse successors).
- **Very few remain:** The number of regulated tenancies has been declining for decades as tenants move on or pass away.

### Protection

A regulated tenant has the right to remain in the property indefinitely (subject to limited grounds for possession) and to have the rent set by the Rent Officer. These tenancies are extremely valuable to the tenant.

## Fixed-Term vs Periodic Tenancies

### Fixed-Term Tenancy

A fixed-term tenancy runs for a **specified period** (commonly 6 or 12 months). Key features:

- **Start and end dates** are stated in the tenancy agreement.
- Neither landlord nor tenant can end the tenancy before the end date unless there is a **break clause** or mutual agreement.
- The landlord cannot increase the rent during the fixed term unless the agreement allows it.
- If the tenant remains after the fixed term expires without signing a new agreement, the tenancy becomes a **statutory periodic tenancy**.

### Periodic Tenancy

A periodic tenancy runs from **period to period** (e.g., month to month, week to week) without a fixed end date. There are two types:

### Contractual Periodic Tenancy

Created by the tenancy agreement itself (e.g., "this tenancy shall be a monthly periodic tenancy"). The terms are those set out in the agreement.

### Statutory Periodic Tenancy

Arises **automatically** under s.5 of the Housing Act 1988 when a fixed-term AST expires and the tenant remains in occupation. The terms mirror the expired fixed-term agreement.

### Ending a Periodic Tenancy

- **Tenant:** Must give at least **1 month's notice** (for monthly tenancies), expiring at the end of a tenancy period.
- **Landlord:** Must use Section 21 (2 months' notice) or Section 8 (variable notice depending on ground).
- Under the RRA 2025, tenants will need to give **2 months' notice** to end a periodic tenancy (not yet in force).

## Joint Tenancies

### What Is a Joint Tenancy?

A joint tenancy exists where **two or more people** are named as tenants on the same tenancy agreement. All joint tenants have equal rights to occupy the whole property.

### Joint and Several Liability

Joint tenants are **jointly and severally liable** for all obligations under the tenancy, most importantly rent. This means:

- The landlord can pursue **any one** joint tenant for the **entire rent**, not just their "share."
- If one joint tenant fails to pay, the others remain liable for the full amount.
- If one joint tenant causes damage, all joint tenants are liable.

### Ending a Joint Tenancy

- **By one tenant:** A periodic joint tenancy can be ended by **any one** joint tenant giving notice to quit, even without the other tenants' agreement (Hammersmith & Fulham LBC v Monk [1992] 1 AC 478). This ends the tenancy for all joint tenants.
- **By agreement:** All joint tenants can agree to surrender the tenancy.
- **Fixed-term:** A fixed-term joint tenancy cannot be ended by one tenant alone before the term expires (unless there is a break clause).

### Domestic Abuse Considerations

Under the Domestic Abuse Act 2021, a victim of domestic abuse who is a joint tenant may be able to:

- Obtain an occupation order excluding the abusive partner.
- Apply for the tenancy to be transferred to them alone.

## Break Clauses

### What Is a Break Clause?

A break clause is a provision in a fixed-term tenancy agreement that allows either the landlord or the tenant (or both) to end the tenancy early, before the fixed term expires.

### Minimum Notice

- A tenant exercising a break clause must give at least **2 months' notice** (this is the statutory minimum under the Housing Act 1988 for landlords, and commonly 1-2 months for tenants as specified in the clause).
- The break clause will specify the earliest date it can be exercised (e.g., "after the first 6 months").
- The tenant must comply strictly with the break clause requirements (e.g., giving notice in writing, ensuring no rent arrears) or the break may be invalid.

### Landlord Break Clauses

A landlord break clause must still comply with Section 21 requirements (including 2 months' notice, deposit protection, etc.). A break clause does not exempt the landlord from statutory protections.

## Lodgers vs Tenants

### The Distinction

The distinction between a lodger and a tenant is legally significant:

- A **lodger** lives in the same property as the landlord, who provides personal services (such as meals, cleaning, or laundry).
- A **tenant** has exclusive possession of defined premises.

### Why It Matters

- **Lodgers** are generally excluded from most tenant protections:
  - No deposit protection requirement.
  - No requirement for Section 21 or Section 8 — the landlord can give "reasonable notice" (usually the rental period).
  - No protection from retaliatory eviction.
  - The Tenant Fees Act 2019 may still apply in some cases.
- **Tenants** have full statutory protections.

### The Test

The key question is whether the occupier has **exclusive possession** of any part of the property. If the landlord retains the right to enter the lodger's room at any time and provides services, the arrangement is more likely a lodger arrangement. If the occupier has a lockable room and the landlord cannot enter without permission, it may be a tenancy regardless of what the agreement says.

## Licence vs Tenancy

### The Legal Distinction

A **licence** gives permission to occupy but does not grant exclusive possession. A **tenancy** (or lease) grants exclusive possession for a term at a rent.

### Street v Mountford [1985] AC 809

The House of Lords established that the substance of the arrangement matters, not the label. If the occupier has:

1. **Exclusive possession** of defined premises,
2. For a **term** (fixed or periodic), and
3. At a **rent**,

then the arrangement is a tenancy, regardless of whether the agreement calls it a "licence."

### Why Landlords Try to Use Licences

Some landlords try to create "licence agreements" to avoid tenant protections (deposit protection, Section 21 requirements, repair obligations). Courts will look through the label to the substance of the arrangement.

### Genuine Licences

Genuine licences include:

- Service occupancies (e.g., a live-in caretaker who must live in the property for their job).
- Hostel and temporary accommodation.
- Family arrangements with no intention to create legal relations.
- Occupiers who genuinely share with the landlord (lodgers).

## Rolling Periodic Tenancies

### What They Are

A rolling periodic tenancy is a tenancy that continues from one rental period to the next (typically month to month) without a fixed end date. It can arise in two ways:

1. **Statutory periodic:** Automatically when a fixed-term AST expires and the tenant stays.
2. **Contractual periodic:** Created by the agreement itself from the outset.

### Advantages for Tenants

- **Flexibility:** The tenant can leave by giving 1 month's notice (for monthly tenancies).
- **Continuity:** The tenancy continues indefinitely until either party takes action.
- **Same protections:** All AST protections (deposit, Section 21 requirements, repair obligations) continue to apply.

### Advantages for Landlords

- No need to renew the tenancy agreement.
- Ability to serve Section 21 at any time (with 2 months' notice).

## The RRA 2025 Transformation (Not Yet in Force)

### All Tenancies Become Periodic

Under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, **fixed-term ASTs will be abolished**. All tenancies will be periodic from day one. The tenant can end the tenancy by giving **2 months' notice** at any time.

### ASTs Become Assured Tenancies

The RRA 2025 will convert ASTs into **assured tenancies**, removing the landlord's ability to use Section 21. This is the most significant change to tenant security since the Housing Act 1988.

### Timeline

- **Phase 1 (expected May 2026):** New tenancies created after the commencement date will be assured periodic tenancies.
- **Phase 2 (expected ~2028):** Existing ASTs will be converted to assured periodic tenancies.
- **Phase 3 (2035-2037):** Full transition complete.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- Most private tenancies are ASTs — check your tenancy agreement to confirm.
- If your fixed term has expired and you are still living in the property, you likely have a statutory periodic tenancy with the same rights.
- Joint tenants are jointly and severally liable for rent — understand the financial risk before entering a joint tenancy.
- If your agreement says "licence" but you have exclusive possession of a room or property, it may legally be a tenancy regardless.
- Lodgers have significantly fewer rights than tenants.
- The RRA 2025 will eventually convert all ASTs to assured tenancies, giving much greater security.
- Break clauses must be exercised strictly in accordance with their terms.
