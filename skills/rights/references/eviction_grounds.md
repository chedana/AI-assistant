# Eviction and Possession Grounds

## Overview

In England, a landlord cannot simply tell a tenant to leave. They must follow a legal process, serve the correct notice, and obtain a court order. The two main routes are Section 21 (no-fault eviction) and Section 8 (fault-based or specific grounds), both under the Housing Act 1988. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 will abolish Section 21 when its provisions come into force.

## Section 21 — No-Fault Eviction (Current Law)

### What Is Section 21?

Section 21 of the Housing Act 1988 allows a landlord to recover possession of a property let on an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST) without giving any reason. It is commonly called a "no-fault" eviction notice.

### Requirements for a Valid Section 21 Notice

A Section 21 notice is only valid if ALL of the following conditions are met:

- The tenancy is an AST.
- The landlord has protected the deposit in a government-approved scheme and served the prescribed information (Housing Act 2004, ss.213-215).
- The landlord has provided the tenant with a valid Energy Performance Certificate (EPC).
- The landlord has provided the tenant with a current Gas Safety Certificate.
- The landlord has provided the tenant with the government's "How to Rent" guide (current edition at the time of the tenancy or renewal).
- The notice is given on the prescribed Form 6A.
- At least **2 months' notice** is given, expiring on or after the last day of a period of the tenancy (for periodic tenancies).
- For fixed-term ASTs, the notice cannot expire before the end of the fixed term (unless there is a break clause).
- No retaliatory eviction protection applies (Deregulation Act 2015, s.33).
- The notice is served within the valid timeframe (within **10 months** of service for court proceedings, previously 6 months before October 2015 tenancies but now standardised).

### Section 21 After Service

After the notice period expires, the landlord must apply to the county court for a possession order. Under the accelerated possession procedure, the court can grant the order without a hearing if the paperwork is in order. The tenant then has **14 days** (or up to 42 days in cases of exceptional hardship) to vacate.

### Abolition Under the Renters' Rights Act 2025

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 (RRA 2025) received Royal Assent in September 2025. **Section 21 will be abolished** when the relevant provisions are commenced. This is expected in **Phase 1 (around May 2026 for new tenancies)** and Phase 2 (around 2028 for existing tenancies). Until commencement, Section 21 remains available. (Not yet in force as of early 2026.)

## Section 8 — Grounds for Possession

### What Is Section 8?

Section 8 of the Housing Act 1988 allows a landlord to seek possession on specific grounds listed in Schedule 2 to the Act. Some grounds are **mandatory** (the court must grant possession if proved) and others are **discretionary** (the court may grant possession if it considers it reasonable).

### Notice Requirements (Current Law)

The landlord must serve a Section 8 notice (Form 3) specifying which grounds are relied upon. The notice period varies by ground:

| Notice Period | Grounds |
|--------------|---------|
| 2 weeks | Grounds 1, 2, 5-7, 9, 16 |
| 2 months | Grounds 1 (if used with periodic), 3, 4 |
| 4 weeks (1 month) | Ground 8 (rent arrears) |
| 2 weeks | Ground 10 (some rent arrears) |
| 2 weeks | Ground 11 (persistent delay in paying rent) |
| 2 weeks | Grounds 12-15 |

### Mandatory Grounds (Court Must Grant Possession)

| Ground | Description | Notice Period |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| **Ground 1** | Landlord previously occupied the property as their only or principal home and wishes to return (must have given prior notice or court considers it just and equitable) | 2 months |
| **Ground 2** | Property subject to a mortgage granted before the tenancy and the mortgagee requires possession | 2 months |
| **Ground 3** | Tenancy is a fixed-term of not more than 8 months and the property was used as a holiday let in the 12 months before the tenancy | 2 months |
| **Ground 4** | Property is part of an educational institution and was let to a student in the 12 months before the tenancy | 2 months |
| **Ground 5** | Property is held for use by a minister of religion and is required for that purpose | 2 months |
| **Ground 6** | Landlord intends to demolish, reconstruct, or carry out substantial works and cannot do so with the tenant in occupation | 2 months |
| **Ground 7** | Tenancy devolved under the will or intestacy of the former tenant (must be sought within 12 months of death) | 2 months |
| **Ground 7A** | Tenant has been convicted of a serious criminal offence, or anti-social behaviour, or breach of an injunction (added by Anti-social Behaviour, Crime and Policing Act 2014) | 4 weeks |
| **Ground 7B** | Tenant's immigration status means they have no right to rent (Immigration Act 2014) | 2 weeks |
| **Ground 8** | At least 2 months' rent arrears at date of notice AND at date of hearing (for monthly tenancies); 8 weeks' for weekly tenancies | 2 weeks |

### Discretionary Grounds (Court May Grant if Reasonable)

| Ground | Description |
|--------|------------|
| **Ground 9** | Suitable alternative accommodation is available for the tenant |
| **Ground 10** | Some rent is unpaid at the date of the notice and the date of the hearing |
| **Ground 11** | Tenant has persistently delayed paying rent, even if no rent is currently due |
| **Ground 12** | Tenant has breached a term of the tenancy (other than rent) |
| **Ground 13** | The condition of the property has deteriorated due to acts of waste by, or neglect/default of, the tenant |
| **Ground 14** | The tenant or a person residing in or visiting the property has been guilty of conduct causing or likely to cause a nuisance or annoyance, or has been convicted of using the property for illegal or immoral purposes |
| **Ground 14A** | The property is occupied by a couple, one of whom has left due to violence or threats of violence by the other, and the court is satisfied the partner who left is unlikely to return |
| **Ground 15** | The condition of furniture provided under the tenancy has deteriorated due to ill-treatment by the tenant |
| **Ground 16** | The tenancy was granted because the tenant was employed by the landlord, and the employment has ended |
| **Ground 17** | The tenant or person acting on their behalf made a false or misleading statement to induce the grant of the tenancy |

## New Grounds Under the Renters' Rights Act 2025

### Ground 1 — Revised (Not Yet in Force)

The RRA 2025 revises Ground 1 so that the landlord must have a genuine intention to occupy the property as their only or principal home. The prior-notice requirement is removed but replaced with stronger safeguards:

- The landlord must give **4 months' notice**.
- Ground 1 cannot be used in the first **12 months** of the tenancy.
- If the landlord re-lets the property within 12 months without moving in, the tenant may seek compensation.

### Ground 1A — Landlord's Close Family Member (Not Yet in Force)

A new mandatory ground allowing possession where the landlord wishes a **close family member** (child, parent, or spouse/partner) to occupy the property as their only or principal home.

- **4 months' notice** required.
- Cannot be used in the first **12 months** of the tenancy.
- Same re-letting penalty as revised Ground 1.

### Ground 6A — Sale of the Property (Not Yet in Force)

A new mandatory ground allowing possession where the landlord intends to **sell the property** (the freehold or grant a lease of 21+ years).

- **4 months' notice** required.
- Cannot be used in the first **12 months** of the tenancy.
- The landlord must market the property for sale.
- If the property is not sold and is re-let within 12 months, the tenant may seek compensation.

### Ground 8A — Repeated Rent Arrears (Not Yet in Force)

A new mandatory ground where the tenant has been in at least **2 months' rent arrears** on three or more occasions within the preceding 3 years, whether or not the arrears persisted at the hearing date. This replaces the rigidity of Ground 8 (which requires arrears at both notice date and hearing date).

### Changes to Existing Grounds Under RRA 2025

- **Ground 6 (redevelopment):** Notice period extended to **4 months**. Cannot be used in the first 12 months.
- **Ground 14 (anti-social behaviour):** The discretionary ground is strengthened; the court can consider behaviour in the locality, not just the property.
- All grounds require the landlord to have given notice using the **new prescribed form** once RRA 2025 is commenced.

## Notice Periods Summary (Post-RRA 2025, Not Yet in Force)

| Ground | Notice Period |
|--------|--------------|
| Grounds 1, 1A, 6, 6A | 4 months |
| Ground 8, 8A | 4 weeks |
| Ground 14 (serious ASB) | 2 weeks |
| Most other grounds | 2 months |

## Illegal Eviction — Protection from Eviction Act 1977

### What Counts as Illegal Eviction

Under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977, it is a **criminal offence** for any person to unlawfully deprive a residential occupier of their occupation. This includes:

- Changing the locks without a court order.
- Physically removing the tenant or their belongings.
- Threatening the tenant to make them leave.
- Disconnecting utilities (gas, electricity, water) to force the tenant out.
- Any act intended to interfere with the tenant's peace, comfort, or convenience to make them give up occupation.

### Penalties

- **Criminal:** Up to 2 years' imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine (s.1 of the 1977 Act).
- **Civil:** The tenant can claim damages under s.27-28 of the Housing Act 1988. Damages are calculated based on the difference in value of the landlord's interest with and without the sitting tenant — this can be very substantial.
- **Injunction:** The county court can grant an emergency injunction to reinstate the tenant.

### Local Authority Enforcement

Local councils have a duty to investigate complaints of illegal eviction and harassment. The council's Tenancy Relations Officer can mediate and, in serious cases, prosecute the landlord.

## The Court Process

### Issuing Proceedings

After the notice period expires, the landlord must apply to the county court for a possession order (Form N5 for Section 8, Form N5B for accelerated Section 21).

### Hearing

- **Section 21 accelerated:** No hearing unless the tenant raises a defence on the papers. If the paperwork is correct, the court grants the order on paper alone.
- **Section 8:** A hearing is almost always required. The landlord must prove the ground. The tenant can defend and raise counterclaims (e.g., disrepair).

### Possession Orders

- **Outright order:** The tenant must leave by a specified date (typically 14 days, up to 42 days for exceptional hardship).
- **Suspended order (discretionary grounds):** The tenant can stay provided they comply with conditions (e.g., paying current rent plus arrears).

### Bailiff Enforcement

If the tenant does not leave by the date in the possession order, the landlord must apply for a warrant of possession. County court bailiffs (or High Court Enforcement Officers, if the order is transferred up) carry out the eviction. The tenant must be given notice of the eviction date.

## Defending Possession Claims

### Common Defences to Section 21

A tenant can defend a Section 21 claim if the notice is invalid. Common invalidity arguments:

- **Deposit not protected** or prescribed information not served (Housing Act 2004, ss.213-215).
- **No valid gas safety certificate** provided to the tenant.
- **No valid EPC** provided to the tenant.
- **"How to Rent" guide** not provided (or wrong version).
- **Wrong form used** (must be Form 6A for ASTs created after 1 October 2015).
- **Insufficient notice period** (must be at least 2 months).
- **Retaliatory eviction** — council served an improvement notice within the preceding 6 months (Deregulation Act 2015, s.33).
- **Notice expired** — court proceedings must be started within 10 months of serving the Section 21 notice.

### Common Defences to Section 8

- **Ground not proved:** The landlord must prove the facts supporting the ground relied upon. For example, Ground 8 requires 2 months' arrears at BOTH the notice date and the hearing date — if the tenant reduces arrears below 2 months before the hearing, Ground 8 fails.
- **Reasonableness (discretionary grounds):** For discretionary grounds, the court must consider whether it is reasonable to grant possession. The tenant's personal circumstances (age, health, children, vulnerability) are relevant.
- **Counterclaim for disrepair:** If the landlord has failed to maintain the property, the tenant can counterclaim for damages, which may offset rent arrears and undermine the possession claim.
- **Procedural defects:** The Section 8 notice must specify the correct grounds and give the correct notice period.

### Legal Aid

Legal aid is still available for housing possession cases in some circumstances. Tenants facing eviction should contact their local Legal Aid provider or Shelter.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- A landlord CANNOT evict you without a court order — any attempt to do so is illegal.
- Always check that a Section 21 notice meets all validity requirements before panicking.
- If you receive a Section 8 notice, seek advice immediately — many grounds are discretionary and can be defended.
- After the RRA 2025 comes into force, Section 21 will no longer be available and all evictions will require specific grounds.
- If you are illegally evicted, call the police (it is a criminal offence) and contact your local council's Tenancy Relations Officer.
- Get legal advice from Shelter, Citizens Advice, or a solicitor specialising in housing law.
