# Pets in Rental Properties and Discrimination

## Overview

Keeping pets in rented accommodation has traditionally been subject to blanket bans in many tenancy agreements. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 introduces a right for tenants to request permission to keep a pet, with landlords required to respond and unable to unreasonably refuse. Separately, discrimination against tenants on grounds of disability, race, gender, benefits status, or family composition is unlawful under the Equality Act 2010 and developing case law.

## Pets in Rented Properties — Current Law

### The Default Position (Pre-RRA 2025)

Currently, there is **no statutory right** to keep a pet in a rented property. The position depends entirely on the tenancy agreement:

- If the agreement contains a **"no pets" clause**, the tenant must comply or risk eviction for breach of tenancy (Section 8, Ground 12).
- If the agreement is **silent** on pets, the tenant can generally keep a pet, but may be liable for any damage caused.
- If the agreement says **"pets with landlord's consent"**, the landlord can withhold consent, but should not do so unreasonably (as a matter of good practice, not law).

### Model Tenancy Agreement

The government's Model Tenancy Agreement (updated 2021) recommends that landlords allow pets by default, with consent not to be unreasonably refused. However, this is only a model — it has no legal force unless the parties adopt it.

## RRA 2025 — Pet Request Rights (Not Yet in Force)

### The New Right

The Renters' Rights Act 2025, s.13-14, introduces a statutory right for tenants to **request permission** to keep a pet in the rental property. This provision is expected to come into force in Phase 1 (around May 2026 for new tenancies).

### The Process

1. **Tenant makes a written request** to the landlord, specifying the type of pet they wish to keep.
2. **Landlord must respond in writing within 42 days** (6 weeks), either:
   - **Granting consent** (with or without conditions), or
   - **Refusing consent**, with **written reasons** for the refusal.
3. If the landlord **fails to respond within 42 days**, consent is deemed to have been granted.

### Reasonable Grounds for Refusal

The landlord can only refuse consent on **reasonable grounds**. What constitutes a reasonable ground will depend on the circumstances but may include:

- The property is too small for the type of animal requested.
- The property has a restrictive covenant or lease term that prohibits pets (e.g., in a leasehold flat where the superior lease bans animals).
- The type of animal poses a genuine health and safety risk.
- A neighbouring tenant has a severe allergy (though this would need to be evidenced).
- The animal is prohibited by law (e.g., a banned breed under the Dangerous Dogs Act 1991).

### What Is NOT a Reasonable Ground

The following are unlikely to be considered reasonable refusals:

- A blanket "no pets" policy without consideration of the specific request.
- Generalised concerns about damage without specific evidence.
- Personal dislike of animals.
- "The property has new carpets" (the landlord can require insurance to cover damage).

### Pet Insurance Requirement

The landlord can, as a condition of granting consent, require the tenant to obtain **pet insurance** that covers:

- Damage to the property caused by the pet.
- Third-party liability (e.g., if the pet injures a visitor or neighbour).

The cost of this insurance is borne by the tenant and is a **permitted payment** under the Tenant Fees Act 2019 (as amended by the RRA 2025).

### Challenging a Refusal

If the tenant believes the landlord's refusal is unreasonable, they can:

- Complain to the **Private Rented Sector Ombudsman** (established by the RRA 2025, not yet in force).
- Apply to the **First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber)** for a determination.

### Existing Pets

The right to request applies to new pet requests. If a tenant already has a pet and the tenancy agreement permits it, the RRA 2025 does not change that position. If the tenancy agreement currently prohibits pets, the tenant can make a fresh request once the RRA 2025 provisions are in force.

## Assistance Dogs and Disability

### Current Law — Equality Act 2010

Under the Equality Act 2010, a landlord **must not** discriminate against a tenant or prospective tenant because of a protected characteristic, including disability (s.4 and s.15).

### Reasonable Adjustments for Assistance Dogs

If a tenant or prospective tenant has a disability and requires an **assistance dog** (guide dog, hearing dog, medical alert dog, or other trained assistance animal), refusing to allow the dog is likely to constitute:

- **Direct discrimination** (s.13 Equality Act 2010) — treating the person less favourably because of their disability.
- **Discrimination arising from disability** (s.15 Equality Act 2010) — treating the person unfavourably because of something arising from their disability (the need for an assistance dog).
- **Failure to make reasonable adjustments** (s.20-21 Equality Act 2010) — the landlord is under a duty to make reasonable adjustments, which would include waiving a "no pets" clause for an assistance dog.

### What Counts as an Assistance Dog

- Dogs certified by **Assistance Dogs UK** member organisations (Guide Dogs, Hearing Dogs, Canine Partners, Dogs for Good, Medical Detection Dogs, Support Dogs, Dog AID).
- Dogs trained by other organisations or owner-trained dogs that perform specific disability-related tasks.
- Emotional support animals (ESAs) do **not** have the same legal status as assistance dogs in the UK. However, a landlord refusing an ESA for a tenant with a mental health disability may still face a discrimination claim if the refusal is connected to the disability.

## Discrimination — Equality Act 2010

### Protected Characteristics

The Equality Act 2010 prohibits discrimination in the provision of housing based on the following **protected characteristics** (s.4):

- Age
- Disability
- Gender reassignment
- Marriage and civil partnership
- Pregnancy and maternity
- Race (including ethnicity, nationality, and national origin)
- Religion or belief
- Sex
- Sexual orientation

### Types of Discrimination in Housing

### Direct Discrimination — s.13

Treating a person less favourably because of a protected characteristic. Examples:

- Refusing to rent to someone because of their race or ethnicity.
- Refusing to rent to someone because they are pregnant.
- Refusing to rent to a same-sex couple.

### Indirect Discrimination — s.19

Applying a provision, criterion, or practice (PCP) that applies to everyone but disproportionately disadvantages people with a protected characteristic, and which cannot be objectively justified. Examples:

- A "no children" policy disproportionately affects women (who are more likely to be primary carers) — potential sex discrimination.
- A "no DSS" (housing benefit) policy disproportionately affects women and disabled people — see below.

### Harassment — s.26

Unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that violates dignity or creates an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment. Examples:

- Racial abuse from a landlord.
- Sexual harassment by a landlord or agent.

### Victimisation — s.27

Treating someone badly because they have made or supported a discrimination complaint.

## "No DSS" — Discrimination Against Benefits Recipients

### The Issue

Many landlords and agents historically advertised properties as "No DSS" (Department of Social Security, now Department for Work and Pensions), meaning they would not accept tenants receiving housing benefit or Universal Credit.

### Case Law: Indirect Discrimination

Two landmark cases established that "No DSS" policies are unlawful:

### York v Patel (2019) — York County Court

District Judge Victoria Mark held that a letting agent's blanket "no DSS" policy constituted unlawful **indirect sex discrimination** under the Equality Act 2010, s.19, because a significantly higher proportion of housing benefit claimants are women.

### R (on the application of) v Fergus Wilson (2020)

A landlord's policy of evicting all tenants on housing benefit was held to be indirectly discriminatory against women and disabled people.

### Shelter and NRLA Guidance

Following these cases:

- **Shelter** confirmed that blanket "no DSS" or "no benefits" policies are likely unlawful.
- The **National Residential Landlords Association (NRLA)** advised landlords to assess each application individually and not apply blanket bans.
- Major property portals (**Rightmove, Zoopla, OnTheMarket**) removed the option to filter by "no DSS" or "no benefits."

### The Legal Position

A landlord may consider a tenant's **financial circumstances** (including affordability), but cannot apply a **blanket ban** based on benefit receipt. Each application must be assessed on its merits. Factors a landlord may consider:

- Whether the rent is affordable (total income vs rent).
- The tenant's credit history and references.
- Whether the tenant has a guarantor.

What the landlord **cannot** do:

- Refuse to accept any tenant who receives benefits.
- Refuse to accept housing benefit or Universal Credit as a source of income without further assessment.
- Apply a "no benefits" policy to advertising.

### Disability Discrimination

"No DSS" policies also disproportionately affect **disabled people**, who are more likely to receive benefits. This constitutes potential indirect **disability discrimination** under s.19 of the Equality Act 2010.

## Discrimination Against Families with Children

### The Law

There is no explicit prohibition on "no children" clauses in the Equality Act 2010 (unlike in some other jurisdictions). However:

### Indirect Sex Discrimination

A "no children" policy may constitute **indirect sex discrimination** under s.19 of the Equality Act 2010, because women are disproportionately primary carers of children. The policy would need to be objectively justified — and it is difficult to see how a blanket "no children" policy could be justified.

### Pregnancy and Maternity Discrimination

Refusing to rent to a pregnant woman, or evicting a tenant because they become pregnant, is **direct discrimination** under s.17 of the Equality Act 2010.

### The Practical Position

While the case law is less developed than for "no DSS" policies, landlords who refuse families with children risk discrimination claims. The safest legal position for landlords is to assess each application on its merits without reference to whether the applicant has children.

## Discrimination Against Other Groups

### Nationality and Immigration

- Landlords must carry out **right-to-rent checks** under the Immigration Act 2014 (checking that the prospective tenant has immigration permission to be in the UK). However, this must not be applied in a discriminatory way — the checks apply equally to all prospective tenants.
- Refusing to rent to someone because of their **nationality** or **ethnic origin** (as opposed to their immigration status) is unlawful race discrimination.

### Age Discrimination

- Refusing to rent to tenants above or below a certain age may constitute **age discrimination** (s.13 Equality Act 2010) unless it can be objectively justified.
- Legitimate justifications may include insurance requirements or property-specific factors.

### Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity

- Refusing to rent to someone because they are LGBTQ+ is unlawful discrimination under the Equality Act 2010.

## Remedies for Discrimination

### County Court

A tenant or prospective tenant who has been discriminated against can bring a claim in the **county court** under Part 4 (Premises) of the Equality Act 2010. Remedies include:

- **Damages** — including compensation for injury to feelings (using the Vento bands, currently £1,200 to £57,000+ depending on severity).
- **Declaration** — that the landlord/agent has acted unlawfully.
- **Injunction** — requiring the landlord/agent to stop the discriminatory practice.

### Time Limit

Claims must be brought within **6 months** of the discriminatory act.

### Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC)

The EHRC can investigate systemic discrimination and take enforcement action against landlords or agents operating discriminatory policies.

### Ombudsman

Under the RRA 2025 (not yet in force), the **Private Rented Sector Ombudsman** will provide an additional avenue for complaints about discrimination by landlords and agents.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- Under the RRA 2025 (when in force), you will have a statutory right to request permission to keep a pet, and the landlord can only refuse on reasonable grounds.
- If you have a disability and need an assistance dog, the landlord must allow it as a reasonable adjustment under the Equality Act 2010 — even if the tenancy agreement says "no pets."
- "No DSS" and "no benefits" policies are likely unlawful indirect discrimination. If you are refused a tenancy solely because you receive benefits, seek legal advice.
- Discrimination based on race, disability, sex, pregnancy, sexual orientation, gender identity, religion, or age is unlawful.
- If you experience discrimination, you can claim damages in the county court within 6 months.
- Document all communications and keep evidence of discriminatory statements or policies.
