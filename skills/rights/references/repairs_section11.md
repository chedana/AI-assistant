# Repairs, Maintenance, and Housing Standards

## Overview

Landlords in England have extensive legal obligations to keep rented properties in good repair and free from serious hazards. The primary legislation is the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 (especially section 11), supplemented by the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018, the Housing Act 2004 (HHSRS), and Awaab's Law (initially for social housing). Tenants have robust remedies including court orders, rent repayment orders, and council enforcement.

## Section 11 — Landlord & Tenant Act 1985

### The Core Obligation

Section 11 of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 implies a covenant into every tenancy of a dwelling-house granted for a term of less than 7 years (which covers virtually all ASTs and periodic tenancies). The landlord **must** keep in repair:

### Structure and Exterior — s.11(1)(a)

- The **structure** of the dwelling: walls, floors, ceilings, roof, foundations, internal structural walls.
- The **exterior** of the dwelling: external walls, doors, windows, window frames, drains, gutters, external pipes.
- This includes damp-proofing, but not cosmetic decoration unless the disrepair makes the property unfit.

### Installations for Water, Gas, Electricity — s.11(1)(b)

- Installations for the supply of **water** — pipes, tanks, cisterns.
- Installations for the supply of **gas** — pipes, flues, ventilation for gas appliances.
- Installations for the supply of **electricity** — wiring, sockets, consumer unit (fuse box).

### Installations for Sanitation, Heating, Hot Water — s.11(1)(c)

- **Sanitation:** Basins, sinks, baths, toilets, and their associated plumbing.
- **Space heating:** Radiators, boilers, central heating systems.
- **Water heating:** Boilers, immersion heaters, hot water cylinders.

### What Section 11 Does NOT Cover

- The tenant's own belongings or appliances (unless provided by the landlord).
- Anything the tenant is responsible for under the tenancy agreement (but the landlord cannot contract out of s.11).
- Damage caused by the tenant's own actions (though the landlord must still repair — they can seek damages from the tenant).
- Rebuilding or reinstatement following damage by fire, flood, or inevitable accident (unless caused by the landlord's breach).
- Internal decoration (unless the disrepair has damaged it, in which case the landlord must make good).

### Landlord's Obligation Is Not Absolute

The landlord's obligation under s.11 only arises once they have **notice** of the disrepair. The tenant must inform the landlord (ideally in writing) of the problem. After notice, the landlord must carry out the repair within a **reasonable time**. What is reasonable depends on the severity: an emergency (burst pipe, no heating in winter) should be addressed within 24 hours; a minor repair might reasonably take a few weeks.

### Cannot Contract Out

Under s.12 of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985, any clause in a tenancy agreement that attempts to exclude or limit the landlord's s.11 obligations is **void**. The tenant cannot be required to do the landlord's repairs.

## Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018

### The Fitness Standard

The Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018 amended the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 by inserting sections 9A-9C. It requires that the property be **fit for human habitation** at the start of the tenancy and **throughout** the tenancy.

### What "Fit for Human Habitation" Means

The Act lists factors from s.10 of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985, including:

- Repair
- Stability
- Freedom from damp
- Internal arrangement
- Natural lighting
- Ventilation
- Water supply
- Drainage and sanitary conveniences
- Facilities for preparation and cooking of food
- Facilities for the disposal of waste water
- Any HHSRS Category 1 or Category 2 hazard

### Tenant's Right to Sue

Unlike s.11 (which requires the landlord to fix specific items), the 2018 Act gives tenants a **direct right to sue** the landlord in the county court for breach of the fitness obligation. The court can order:

- The landlord to carry out works to make the property fit.
- Damages for the period the property was unfit.
- A reduction in rent.

### Applies to All Tenancies

The 2018 Act applies to tenancies granted on or after 20 March 2019 (and to pre-existing tenancies from 20 March 2020). It covers properties regardless of rent level.

## HHSRS — Housing Health and Safety Rating System

### Housing Act 2004, Part 1

The Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) is a risk-based assessment framework under Part 1 of the Housing Act 2004. Local councils use it to assess housing conditions.

### Category 1 Hazards

Category 1 hazards are serious and the council has a **duty** to take action. They include:

- **Excess cold** — inadequate heating or insulation.
- **Damp and mould growth** — penetrating damp, rising damp, condensation causing mould.
- **Falls** — dangerous stairs, uneven floors, missing banisters.
- **Fire** — inadequate fire detection, blocked escape routes.
- **Electrical hazards** — exposed wiring, defective sockets.
- **Structural collapse** — serious structural defects.
- **Crowding and space** — insufficient living space.

### Category 2 Hazards

Category 2 hazards are less serious, and the council has a **power** (but not a duty) to take action. Action is at the council's discretion.

### Council Enforcement Powers

When a council identifies a Category 1 hazard, it must take one of the following actions under the Housing Act 2004:

- **Improvement notice (s.11-12):** Requires the landlord to carry out specified works within a set timeframe.
- **Prohibition order (s.20-21):** Prohibits the use of the property (or part of it) until the hazard is remedied.
- **Hazard awareness notice (s.28-29):** Advises the landlord of the hazard (least interventionist).
- **Emergency remedial action (s.40):** The council itself carries out works and charges the landlord.
- **Emergency prohibition order (s.43):** Immediate prohibition of use.
- **Demolition order** (Housing Act 2004, s.265): In the most extreme cases.

## Awaab's Law — Social Housing Damp and Mould

### Background

Awaab's Law is named after Awaab Ishak, a two-year-old who died in December 2020 from a respiratory condition caused by prolonged exposure to mould in his family's social housing in Rochdale. The Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023 introduced "Awaab's Law" amendments.

### Requirements (Social Housing Only — Currently)

Awaab's Law sets specific timescales for social housing landlords (housing associations and councils) to address damp, mould, and condensation:

- **Investigation:** The landlord must investigate within **14 calendar days** of being notified of a potential hazard.
- **Repair:** If a hazard is confirmed, repairs must begin within **7 calendar days** and be completed within a reasonable further period.
- **Emergency repairs:** Where there is an imminent risk to health, the landlord must make the property safe within **24 hours**.

### Extension to Private Rented Sector

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 contains a provision (s.12) to extend Awaab's Law timescales to the **private rented sector** through secondary legislation. (Not yet in force — the Secretary of State must make regulations setting specific timescales for private landlords.) This is expected to be implemented after the initial RRA 2025 provisions come into force.

## Tenant's Right to Repair

### Secure Tenancies (Council Tenants)

Council tenants have a statutory "Right to Repair" under the Secure Tenants of Local Housing Authorities (Right to Repair) Regulations 1994. If the council fails to carry out a qualifying repair within a prescribed timescale, the tenant can have the work done by another contractor and the council must pay.

### Private Tenants — Common Law

Private tenants do not have an equivalent statutory right, but they can:

1. **Notify the landlord in writing** of the disrepair (always keep copies).
2. **Give the landlord a reasonable time** to respond and carry out repairs.
3. If the landlord fails to act, **report to the local council** who can inspect and serve an improvement notice.
4. **Apply to the county court** for an order requiring the landlord to carry out repairs, plus damages.
5. In extreme cases (but with caution), **carry out emergency repairs and deduct the cost from rent** — this is permitted at common law (Lee v Leeds City Council) but should only be done after proper notice and where the repair is urgent. Legal advice is strongly recommended first.

### Important: Do NOT Withhold Rent

While it is tempting, **withholding rent** is generally NOT advisable as a response to disrepair. The landlord can use rent arrears as a ground for possession (Ground 8 or 10). Instead, continue paying rent and pursue the remedies above.

## Rent Repayment Orders

### Housing Act 2004, ss.73-74 and Housing and Planning Act 2016

If a landlord commits certain housing offences, the tenant can apply to the **First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber)** for a Rent Repayment Order (RRO). An RRO requires the landlord to repay up to **12 months' rent** to the tenant.

### Qualifying Offences

- Failure to comply with an improvement notice (Housing Act 2004, s.30).
- Failure to licence an HMO (Housing Act 2004, s.72).
- Failure to comply with a prohibition order (Housing Act 2004, s.32).
- Using violence to secure entry (Criminal Law Act 1977, s.6).
- Illegal eviction or harassment (Protection from Eviction Act 1977, s.1).
- Breach of a banning order (Housing and Planning Act 2016).

### How to Apply

The tenant applies directly to the First-tier Tribunal. Legal aid may be available. The Tribunal considers the landlord's conduct, the tenant's circumstances, and whether the tenant was partly at fault. The Tribunal has discretion over the amount, up to the maximum of 12 months' rent.

## Council Enforcement

### How to Involve Your Local Council

1. **Contact the council's Environmental Health or Private Sector Housing team.**
2. Request an **inspection** of the property under the HHSRS.
3. The council inspector will assess the property and identify any hazards.
4. If Category 1 hazards are found, the council **must** take enforcement action.
5. If Category 2 hazards are found, the council **may** take action.

### Council Powers

- **Improvement notices** — requiring specific works within a set timescale.
- **Prohibition orders** — banning use of the property until works are done.
- **Civil penalties** — up to **£30,000** per offence (as an alternative to prosecution) under the Housing and Planning Act 2016, s.126.
- **Prosecution** — criminal prosecution for non-compliance.
- **Works in default** — the council carries out works and charges the landlord.

### Retaliatory Eviction Protection

If the tenant complains to the council about disrepair and the council serves an improvement notice, the tenant is protected from retaliatory eviction under the Deregulation Act 2015, s.33 (see separate reference on retaliatory eviction).

## Electrical Safety Standards

### Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020

- Landlords must ensure that **electrical installations** are inspected and tested by a qualified person at least every **5 years**.
- An **Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR)** must be provided to the tenant before they occupy the property (for new tenancies) or within 28 days of the inspection.
- If the EICR identifies faults, the landlord must carry out remedial works within **28 days** (or the timescale specified in the report).
- The local council can impose a civil penalty of up to **£30,000** for non-compliance.

## Gas Safety

### Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998

- Landlords must have all gas appliances, fittings, and flues checked by a **Gas Safe registered engineer** every **12 months**.
- A copy of the **Gas Safety Certificate (CP12)** must be given to the tenant within **28 days** of the check, or before the tenant moves in (for new tenancies).
- Failure to comply is a criminal offence and can result in prosecution, unlimited fines, and imprisonment.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- Your landlord is legally required to keep the structure, exterior, and installations in repair — this cannot be overridden by your tenancy agreement.
- Always report disrepair in writing (email is fine) and keep copies of all correspondence.
- If your landlord fails to act, contact your local council's Environmental Health team.
- You have a right to live in a property that is fit for human habitation.
- You can claim compensation (damages) for disrepair through the county court.
- Never withhold rent — use the proper legal channels instead.
- For emergencies (no heating, no hot water, gas leak), the landlord should respond within 24 hours.
