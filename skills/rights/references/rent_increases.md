# Rent Increases

## Overview

Rent increases in England are governed by a combination of the tenancy agreement and statute. For periodic tenancies, the Section 13 procedure under the Housing Act 1988 provides a structured mechanism. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 will introduce significant reforms to protect tenants from excessive increases and ban bidding wars. Rent review clauses in fixed-term agreements are also subject to legal constraints.

## Fixed-Term Tenancies

### During the Fixed Term

During a fixed-term tenancy (e.g., a 12-month AST), the landlord **cannot** increase the rent unless:

1. The tenant **agrees** to the increase (a new agreement or variation is signed), or
2. The tenancy agreement contains a **rent review clause** that specifies when and how the rent can be increased.

Without either of these, the rent remains fixed for the duration of the term.

### Rent Review Clauses

A rent review clause in a fixed-term tenancy is enforceable if:

- It clearly specifies **when** the review takes place (e.g., annually on a specific date).
- It sets out **how** the new rent will be determined (e.g., linked to a specific index, or "to market rent").
- It does not amount to an unfair term under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 (e.g., giving the landlord unlimited discretion to set any rent they choose may be challenged as unfair).

### At the End of the Fixed Term

When a fixed-term AST expires and the tenant remains in occupation, the tenancy typically becomes a **statutory periodic tenancy** (Housing Act 1988, s.5). The rent remains the same until the landlord uses the Section 13 procedure or agrees a new fixed term at a different rent.

## Section 13 — Rent Increases for Periodic Tenancies

### Housing Act 1988, s.13

Section 13 provides a statutory mechanism for landlords to increase the rent on a periodic tenancy (including statutory periodic tenancies that arise when a fixed term expires).

### Current Procedure (Pre-RRA 2025)

1. The landlord serves a **Section 13 notice** (prescribed Form 4) on the tenant.
2. The notice must specify the **new rent** and the **date from which it takes effect**.
3. The minimum notice period is currently:
   - **1 month** for monthly or shorter periodic tenancies.
   - **6 months** for yearly periodic tenancies.
4. The increase cannot take effect earlier than **12 months** after the last rent increase (or the start of the tenancy if no previous increase).
5. The notice cannot take effect before the end of the first period of the tenancy.

### Tenant's Right to Challenge

If the tenant considers the proposed rent increase is above **market rent**, they can apply to the **First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber)** within the notice period. The Tribunal will determine the **open market rent** for the property, considering:

- The condition of the property.
- The terms of the tenancy.
- Comparable rents for similar properties in the area.
- NOT the personal circumstances of the landlord or tenant.

Currently, the Tribunal can determine a rent that is **higher** than the landlord proposed, which discourages some tenants from challenging increases.

## RRA 2025 — Rent Increase Reforms (Not Yet in Force)

### Two-Month Notice Period

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 increases the minimum notice period for a Section 13 rent increase to **2 months** for all periodic tenancies. This gives tenants more time to prepare or challenge.

### Once Per Year Only

Under the RRA 2025, rent can only be increased **once every 12 months** via the Section 13 process. This codifies and strengthens the existing practice.

### Tribunal Can Only Determine Market Rent, Not Higher

Critically, the RRA 2025 amends the First-tier Tribunal's power so that it can **only determine the market rent or lower** — it can **no longer set a rent higher** than the landlord originally proposed. This removes the chilling effect that previously discouraged tenants from challenging increases.

### Section 13 Becomes the Only Mechanism

Under the RRA 2025, the Section 13 notice becomes the **sole mechanism** for rent increases (other than by mutual agreement). Rent review clauses that allow increases outside the Section 13 procedure will be **void**. This prevents landlords from using contractual clauses to bypass the statutory protections.

### Effective Date

These rent increase reforms will come into force when the relevant provisions of the RRA 2025 are commenced, expected in Phase 1 (around May 2026 for new tenancies). Until then, the current rules apply.

## Bidding Wars — Banned Under RRA 2025 (Not Yet in Force)

### What Are Bidding Wars?

In competitive rental markets, letting agents and landlords sometimes encourage prospective tenants to bid above the asking rent. This practice disproportionately affects those on lower incomes and can drive rents above market levels.

### The RRA 2025 Ban

The Renters' Rights Act 2025, s.58, makes it an offence for a landlord or letting agent to:

- **Invite or encourage** prospective tenants to offer more than the advertised rent.
- **Accept** rent higher than the advertised amount (unless the tenant offered it entirely unprompted and without any encouragement).

### Enforcement

- The ban will be enforced by local councils and the new **Private Rented Sector Ombudsman** (also established by the RRA 2025).
- Penalties include **civil penalties of up to £7,000** for a first offence and **up to £40,000** for repeat offences, or criminal prosecution.

### Advertising Requirements

Under the RRA 2025, landlords and agents must state the rent in any property advertisement. They must not advertise a property at one rent and then seek higher offers.

## Challenging an Unfair Rent Increase

### Step 1: Check the Notice

- Is it on the correct form (Form 4)?
- Does it give the correct notice period?
- Has there been at least 12 months since the last increase?
- Does it specify the correct start date?

If any of these are wrong, the notice may be **invalid**.

### Step 2: Negotiate

Many landlords will negotiate. Gather evidence of comparable rents in the area (from property listings, Valuation Office Agency data, or rent indices). Present this to the landlord with a counter-proposal.

### Step 3: Apply to the Tribunal

If negotiation fails, apply to the First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber) **before** the increase takes effect. The application is **free**. The Tribunal will inspect the property (or assess evidence) and determine the market rent.

### Step 4: Tribunal Determination

- The Tribunal's determination replaces the landlord's proposed rent.
- Under current law, the Tribunal can set the rent higher than proposed (a risk).
- Under the RRA 2025 (when in force), the Tribunal can only set the rent at or below the landlord's proposal.
- The determination takes effect from the date specified in the Section 13 notice.

## Rent Increases in the Private Sector — Practical Guidance

### What Is "Market Rent"?

Market rent is the rent that a willing tenant would pay and a willing landlord would accept, for a tenancy of the same property on the same terms, in the open market. It is NOT:

- The highest rent anyone would pay.
- The rent the landlord needs to cover their mortgage.
- The rent adjusted for improvements the tenant has made (tenant improvements are excluded from the valuation).

### How Often Can Rent Be Increased?

| Situation | Current Law | After RRA 2025 |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Fixed term | Only if the tenancy agreement allows it | Only via s.13 or mutual agreement |
| Periodic (monthly) | Once every 12 months, 1 month notice | Once every 12 months, 2 months notice |
| Periodic (yearly) | Once every 12 months, 6 months notice | Once every 12 months, 2 months notice |

### Common Landlord Tactics

- **"Agree to this increase or I'll serve a Section 21."** Under current law, this is technically possible but morally questionable. Under the RRA 2025 (when in force), Section 21 will be abolished, removing this threat.
- **"Your rent is below market rate."** Check comparable rents. If true, a moderate increase may be reasonable. If not, challenge it.
- **"I need to increase rent to cover my costs."** The landlord's costs are not relevant to market rent. The Tribunal does not consider the landlord's mortgage payments, insurance, or maintenance costs.

## Rent Repayment Orders for Excessive Charges

### Tenant Fees Act 2019 Connection

If a landlord charges fees or payments prohibited by the Tenant Fees Act 2019 (disguised as "rent increases" or "administration fees"), the tenant can apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a rent repayment order and recovery of the prohibited payment.

## Social Housing Rent

### Rent Standard

Social housing rents (council and housing association properties) are regulated by the **Rent Standard** set by the Regulator of Social Housing. For 2024-2025, rents can increase by a maximum of **CPI + 1%** (Consumer Prices Index plus one percentage point). This cap does not apply to private sector rents.

## Rent-to-Rent and Guaranteed Rent Schemes

### What Are They?

In "rent-to-rent" arrangements, a third party (a company or individual) leases a property from the landlord on a long-term basis and then sublets it to tenants, often at a higher rent. In "guaranteed rent" schemes, a company guarantees the landlord a fixed rental income and manages the property.

### Tenant Implications

- The tenant's direct landlord is the rent-to-rent operator, not the property owner.
- The tenant still has full statutory protections (deposit protection, s.11 repairs, etc.).
- If the rent-to-rent operator fails to protect the deposit, the **tenant can claim against both** the operator and the property owner.
- Rent increases by the operator must follow the Section 13 procedure if the tenancy is periodic.

## Rent Reduction Claims

### When Can Rent Be Reduced?

Tenants may be entitled to a **rent reduction** (or damages equivalent to a reduction) where:

- The property is in **disrepair** and the landlord has failed to address it after notice — the tenant can claim damages for the reduced value of the property during the period of disrepair.
- The property is **not fit for human habitation** under s.9A of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985.
- There has been **loss of amenity** caused by the landlord (e.g., loss of a garden, loss of parking space that was included in the tenancy).

### How Rent Reduction Is Calculated

Courts typically assess rent reduction as a **percentage** of the rent, reflecting the proportion of the property or its amenity that has been lost. For example:

- Unusable bedroom due to damp: 15-30% reduction.
- No heating in winter: 25-50% reduction.
- Total loss of hot water: 20-35% reduction.
- Persistent leak affecting one room: 10-20% reduction.

These percentages are illustrative; each case depends on its facts.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- During a fixed term, your rent cannot be increased unless your tenancy agreement specifically allows it.
- For periodic tenancies, the landlord must use the Section 13 procedure with proper notice.
- You can challenge any proposed increase at the First-tier Tribunal — the application is free.
- Under the RRA 2025 (when in force), the Tribunal will no longer be able to set the rent higher than the landlord proposed, making challenges risk-free.
- Bidding wars will be banned under the RRA 2025.
- Rent can only be increased once every 12 months.
- Always check comparable rents in your area before accepting an increase.
- Keep records of all rent increase notices and correspondence.
