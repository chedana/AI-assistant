# Renters' Rights Act 2025 — Implementation Timeline

## Overview

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 (RRA 2025) received Royal Assent on 19 September 2025. It is the most significant reform of private renting in England since the Housing Act 1988. The Act's provisions will be brought into force in phases through commencement orders made by the Secretary of State. As of early 2026, no provisions have yet been commenced — the Act is on the statute book but its key reforms are not yet in force.

## Background and Legislative History

### The Journey to Royal Assent

- **2019:** The Conservative government's "A New Deal for Renting" white paper proposed abolishing Section 21.
- **2022-2023:** The Renters (Reform) Bill was introduced but fell at the dissolution of Parliament for the 2024 general election.
- **2024:** The Labour government introduced the **Renters' Rights Bill**, a strengthened version of the earlier bill.
- **19 September 2025:** The Renters' Rights Act 2025 received Royal Assent.

### Key Differences from the Renters (Reform) Bill

The RRA 2025 goes further than the earlier Conservative bill in several ways:

- **Immediate application to new tenancies** — the earlier bill proposed a two-stage transition; the RRA 2025 accelerates this.
- **Stronger rent increase protections** — the Tribunal can no longer set rent higher than the landlord proposed.
- **Bidding wars ban** — explicitly prohibited (not in the earlier bill).
- **More robust Ombudsman powers.**
- **Property Portal** made publicly accessible.

## Phase 1 — New Tenancies (Expected May 2026)

### What Is Phase 1?

Phase 1 is expected to apply to **new tenancies** created on or after the commencement date (anticipated around May 2026, though the exact date depends on the commencement order). The government has indicated it will give landlords and agents at least 6 months' notice before commencement.

### Provisions Expected in Phase 1

| Provision | Section | Description |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Section 21 abolition** | s.1 | No-fault eviction abolished for new tenancies |
| **Periodic tenancies only** | s.2-3 | Fixed terms abolished; all new tenancies are periodic from day one |
| **Tenant 2-month notice** | s.4 | Tenants can end tenancy with 2 months' notice at any time |
| **New eviction grounds** | s.5-7, Sch.1 | Ground 1 revised (landlord moving in), Ground 1A (family), Ground 6A (sale), Ground 8A (repeated arrears) |
| **12-month protected period** | s.5 | Grounds 1, 1A, 6, and 6A cannot be used in first 12 months |
| **Rent increase reform** | s.8-9 | Section 13 only; 2-month notice; Tribunal cannot set rent above landlord's proposal |
| **Bidding wars ban** | s.58 | Illegal to invite or accept offers above advertised rent |
| **Pet requests** | s.13-14 | Tenant right to request pets; landlord must respond within 42 days |
| **Landlord Ombudsman** | s.28-35 | Private Rented Sector Ombudsman established; all landlords must join |
| **Property Portal** | s.36-42 | All landlords must register properties; publicly accessible database |
| **Decent Homes Standard** | s.10-11 | Decent Homes Standard applied to private rented sector |
| **Awaab's Law extension** | s.12 | Power to set timescales for private landlords to address hazards |
| **Anti-discrimination** | s.57 | Landlords cannot blanket-refuse benefit recipients |

### What This Means for New Tenants (Phase 1)

- Your tenancy will be periodic from the start — no fixed term.
- You can leave with 2 months' notice at any time.
- Your landlord cannot evict you without a specific ground.
- Your landlord cannot increase rent more than once a year, with 2 months' notice, and you can challenge any increase at the Tribunal risk-free.
- You can request permission to keep a pet.

## Phase 2 — Existing Tenancies (Expected ~2028)

### What Is Phase 2?

Phase 2 will extend the RRA 2025 provisions to **existing tenancies** (ASTs that were created before the Phase 1 commencement date). This is expected around 2028, though the exact date will depend on the government's assessment of the sector's readiness.

### What Happens to Existing ASTs

- **Section 21 abolished** for existing tenancies — landlords can no longer serve no-fault eviction notices.
- **Existing fixed terms continue** until they expire, at which point they become periodic tenancies under the new regime.
- **All other RRA 2025 provisions** (pet requests, rent increase reforms, Ombudsman, etc.) will apply to existing tenancies.

### Transitional Protections

- Section 21 notices served before the Phase 2 commencement date remain valid if court proceedings are issued before a specified cut-off date.
- Existing rent review clauses in fixed-term agreements continue until the fixed term expires.
- The government will publish detailed transitional guidance.

## Phase 3 — Full Transition (2035-2037)

### Complete Transition to Assured Tenancies

By Phase 3, the transition from ASTs to assured periodic tenancies will be **complete**:

- All remaining ASTs will have been converted.
- The concept of "Assured Shorthold Tenancy" will be obsolete.
- The private rented sector will operate entirely under the new framework.

### Timeline Reasoning

The extended timeline reflects:

- Some fixed-term ASTs have terms running to 2030 or beyond.
- The government aims to avoid disruption by allowing existing fixed terms to expire naturally.
- By 2035-2037, virtually all pre-RRA 2025 fixed terms will have expired.

## The Private Rented Sector Ombudsman

### What Is It?

The RRA 2025 establishes a **Private Rented Sector Ombudsman** — an independent body that tenants can complain to about their landlord's conduct. All private landlords in England will be required to join the Ombudsman scheme.

### Powers

- Investigate complaints from tenants.
- Make binding decisions.
- Order landlords to pay **compensation** to tenants.
- Order landlords to take specific **remedial action**.
- Issue **compliance notices**.
- Publish details of non-compliant landlords.

### Scope of Complaints

The Ombudsman will handle complaints about:

- Disrepair and housing conditions.
- Harassment and poor management.
- Unreasonable refusal of pet requests.
- Unfair treatment (including discrimination).
- Failure to comply with statutory obligations.

## The Property Portal

### What Is It?

The RRA 2025 establishes a **Property Portal** — a national, publicly accessible database of all private rented properties and their landlords. All private landlords must register themselves and their properties.

### What It Will Contain

- Landlord identification and contact details.
- Property addresses.
- Compliance information (gas safety, EPC, deposit protection, electrical safety).
- Ombudsman membership status.
- Any enforcement actions taken against the landlord.

### Purpose

- Enable tenants to **check landlord compliance** before renting.
- Enable councils to **identify non-compliant landlords** for enforcement.
- Replace the existing Database of Rogue Landlords with a comprehensive public system.

## Preparing for the RRA 2025

### For Tenants

- Understand which provisions are not yet in force — do not rely on rights that have not been commenced.
- Continue to use existing protections (deposit protection, Section 21 validity checks, retaliatory eviction defences, Tenant Fees Act).
- When Phase 1 commences, new tenancies will automatically benefit from the new rules.

### Current Protections Still Apply

Until the RRA 2025 provisions are commenced, all existing protections remain in full force:

- Housing Act 2004 (deposit protection, HHSRS).
- Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 (s.11 repairs).
- Protection from Eviction Act 1977 (harassment, illegal eviction).
- Tenant Fees Act 2019 (banned fees).
- Deregulation Act 2015 (retaliatory eviction).
- Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018.

## Key Takeaways

- The RRA 2025 is law but its provisions are not yet in force — commencement orders are needed.
- Phase 1 (new tenancies, expected May 2026) will bring the biggest immediate changes: Section 21 abolition, periodic-only tenancies, pet rights, rent reforms, bidding wars ban, Ombudsman, and Property Portal.
- Phase 2 (existing tenancies, expected ~2028) will extend all provisions to current ASTs.
- Until commencement, existing tenant protections continue to apply fully.
- The transition will not be instantaneous — it will roll out over several years to allow the sector to adapt.
