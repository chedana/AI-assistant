# Tenancy Deposits and Protection

## Overview

In England, landlords and letting agents must protect tenancy deposits in a government-approved scheme within 30 days of receiving them. The key legislation is the Housing Act 2004 (sections 213-215) and the Tenant Fees Act 2019 (which caps deposit amounts). Failure to comply carries significant penalties and restricts the landlord's ability to end the tenancy.

## Deposit Cap — Tenant Fees Act 2019

### Maximum Deposit Amount

Under the Tenant Fees Act 2019, s.3 and Schedule 1, the maximum tenancy deposit a landlord or agent can require is:

- **5 weeks' rent** where the total annual rent is less than £50,000.
- **6 weeks' rent** where the total annual rent is £50,000 or more.

"Weeks' rent" is calculated as: (monthly rent x 12) / 52.

### Example Calculations

| Monthly Rent | Annual Rent | Max Deposit (5 weeks) |
|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| £1,000 | £12,000 | £1,153.85 |
| £1,500 | £18,000 | £1,730.77 |
| £2,000 | £24,000 | £2,307.69 |

### Holding Deposits

Holding deposits are separate from tenancy deposits and are capped at **1 week's rent** under the Tenant Fees Act 2019, Schedule 1, para 3. They must be returned within 15 days of receiving it if the tenancy does not proceed, unless the tenant has provided false or misleading information, has failed a right-to-rent check, or has pulled out of the tenancy.

## The Three Government-Approved Schemes

### Deposit Protection Service (DPS)

- **Type:** Custodial (free) and Insured options.
- **Custodial model:** The DPS holds the deposit money itself. Free to use.
- **Website:** depositprotection.com
- The most commonly used custodial scheme.

### MyDeposits

- **Type:** Custodial and Insured options.
- **Custodial model:** MyDeposits holds the deposit. Free to landlords.
- **Insured model:** Landlord retains the deposit but pays an insurance premium.
- **Website:** mydeposits.co.uk

### Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)

- **Type:** Custodial and Insured options.
- **Custodial model:** TDS holds the deposit. Free to use.
- **Insured model:** Landlord retains the deposit and pays a fee.
- **Website:** tenancydepositscheme.com

### Custodial vs Insured

- **Custodial:** The scheme holds the deposit money. Free for landlords. The deposit is held independently and returned according to the scheme's dispute resolution.
- **Insured:** The landlord holds the deposit money but pays the scheme a fee for insurance. If the landlord fails to return the deposit, the scheme pays the tenant and pursues the landlord.

## 30-Day Protection Deadline

### Housing Act 2004, s.213

Under s.213(3)-(6), the landlord must protect the deposit in a government-approved scheme within **30 days** of receiving it. This 30-day period begins on the date the landlord or agent receives the deposit, not the tenancy start date.

### Prescribed Information — s.213(5)-(6)

Within the same 30-day period, the landlord must also provide the tenant with "prescribed information" including:

- Which scheme the deposit is protected with.
- The scheme's contact details and dispute resolution process.
- The landlord's or agent's contact details.
- The property address.
- The amount of the deposit.
- How to apply for the deposit's return.
- What to do if there is a dispute.
- The purpose of the deposit.

The prescribed information must be given in the form required by the Housing (Tenancy Deposits) (Prescribed Information) Order 2007 (SI 2007/797).

## Penalties for Non-Protection

### Housing Act 2004, s.214

If a landlord fails to protect the deposit or provide the prescribed information within 30 days, the tenant (or former tenant) can apply to the county court under s.214. The court **must** order:

- The deposit to be returned to the tenant, **or** the deposit to be protected in a scheme within 14 days.
- Payment of compensation of between **1x and 3x the deposit amount** to the tenant.

### Multiple Penalties

If the deposit remains unprotected and the tenant makes a second application, the court must again award 1-3x the deposit. There is no limit on the number of times a tenant can apply while the deposit remains unprotected.

### Section 21 Restriction

Under s.215 of the Housing Act 2004:

- A landlord **cannot serve a valid Section 21 notice** while the deposit is not protected in a scheme.
- A landlord **cannot serve a valid Section 21 notice** if the prescribed information has not been given.
- Even if the deposit is later protected, the landlord must wait until the deposit is properly protected and prescribed information served before a Section 21 notice is valid.

This is one of the most powerful tenant protections: non-compliance with deposit rules blocks the landlord's primary no-fault eviction route.

## Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)

### Free ADR Through Schemes

All three government-approved deposit protection schemes offer **free Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)** for disputes at the end of the tenancy. Both landlord and tenant must agree to use ADR; it is not compulsory unless both parties consent.

### ADR Process

1. The tenancy ends and the tenant requests their deposit back.
2. If the landlord proposes deductions and the tenant disagrees, either party can raise a dispute with the scheme.
3. Both parties submit evidence (check-in/check-out reports, photos, invoices, correspondence).
4. An independent adjudicator reviews the evidence and makes a binding decision.
5. The scheme releases the deposit according to the adjudicator's decision.

### Time Limits

- The tenant or landlord typically has **3 months** from the tenancy end date to raise a dispute with the scheme (varies by scheme).
- If no dispute is raised and both parties agree on the split, the deposit is released accordingly.

### Going to Court Instead

Either party can choose to go to the county court instead of using ADR. Court proceedings use the standard small claims track (for claims under £10,000) or fast track.

## Permitted Deductions

### What Landlords Can Deduct

A landlord can only make deductions from the deposit for:

- **Unpaid rent** — any rent arrears at the end of the tenancy.
- **Damage beyond fair wear and tear** — the tenant is liable for damage they (or their guests) caused, but NOT for reasonable wear and tear.
- **Cleaning costs** — only if the property was left significantly dirtier than when the tenant moved in. The property cannot be expected to be returned in a better condition than at the start.
- **Missing items** — if an inventory was provided and items are missing.
- **Breach of tenancy agreement** — e.g., unauthorised alterations.

### Fair Wear and Tear

"Fair wear and tear" means the reasonable deterioration that occurs through normal everyday use. Examples:

- Faded curtains from sunlight = fair wear and tear.
- Worn carpet in high-traffic areas = fair wear and tear.
- Holes punched in walls = NOT fair wear and tear.
- Cigarette burns on carpet = NOT fair wear and tear.

### The Importance of Inventories

A detailed check-in inventory with photos is essential. Without one, the landlord will struggle to prove the condition of the property at the start of the tenancy, and ADR adjudicators typically find in the tenant's favour when no inventory exists.

## End-of-Tenancy Deposit Return Process

### Timeline

1. **Tenancy ends** — tenant returns keys and vacates.
2. **Check-out inspection** — ideally conducted jointly or by an independent inventory clerk.
3. **Landlord proposes deductions (if any)** — should do so promptly, ideally within 10 days.
4. **Tenant agrees or disputes** — if agreed, both parties confirm with the scheme and the deposit is released.
5. **If disputed** — either party can invoke ADR or go to court.

### Scheme Release Timescales

- **DPS custodial:** Aims to return agreed amounts within 10 working days.
- **MyDeposits:** Similar 10-working-day target.
- **TDS:** Similar 10-working-day target.

### If Both Parties Cannot Agree

If there is no agreement after a reasonable period, the scheme will hold the disputed amount until either ADR is completed or a court order is obtained. The undisputed portion should be released promptly.

## Deposits Paid Before 6 April 2007

The deposit protection regime came into force on 6 April 2007 for new ASTs. For deposits received before this date on tenancies that have since become statutory periodic, the position is complex. The Supreme Court in Superstrike Ltd v Rodrigues [2013] UKSC 74 held that a new tenancy (the statutory periodic) arises, meaning the deposit must be protected. Landlords holding pre-2007 deposits on periodic tenancies must protect them.

## Deposit Disputes — Common Scenarios

### Landlord Claims the Whole Deposit

If a landlord attempts to retain the entire deposit, the tenant should:

1. Request an **itemised breakdown** of all proposed deductions with supporting evidence (invoices, photos, quotes).
2. Compare the claims against the **check-in inventory** — deductions are only valid if the landlord can demonstrate deterioration beyond fair wear and tear.
3. If the tenant disagrees, invoke the **ADR process** through the deposit protection scheme.
4. Adjudicators consistently require the landlord to prove their case — the **burden of proof** is on the landlord, not the tenant.

### Landlord Has Not Protected the Deposit

If the tenant discovers (e.g., by searching all three scheme websites) that the deposit was never protected:

1. Raise the issue with the landlord in writing and request immediate protection.
2. If the landlord does not comply, apply to the **county court** under s.214 of the Housing Act 2004.
3. The court must order compensation of **1-3x the deposit amount**.
4. Any Section 21 notice the landlord serves is **invalid** until the deposit is properly protected.

### Agent Holding the Deposit

Where a letting agent received the deposit, the obligation to protect it remains. If the agent goes into administration or absconds with the deposit, the **landlord remains liable** — the tenant's claim is against the landlord, who must ensure protection regardless of who physically holds the money.

### Deposit Held in the Wrong Name

The deposit must be protected under the correct landlord's name and the correct property address. If the details are wrong (e.g., the landlord has changed, or the address is incorrect), the protection may be invalid and the tenant may have a claim under s.214.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- Your deposit must be protected within 30 days — ask your landlord which scheme it is in.
- You are entitled to the prescribed information in writing.
- If your deposit is not protected, you can claim 1-3x the deposit amount in court.
- An unprotected deposit means any Section 21 eviction notice is invalid.
- Use the free ADR service if you dispute deductions — it is quicker and cheaper than court.
- Take your own dated photos at check-in and check-out.
- Fair wear and tear deductions are not permitted.
