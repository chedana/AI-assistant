# General Tenant Rights in England

## Overview

Tenants in England are protected by a comprehensive framework of legislation, common law, and regulation. This reference covers the key Acts every tenant should know, landlord and tenant obligations, HMO rules, subletting, safety certificates, and energy performance requirements. It serves as a catch-all for important topics not covered in depth in the specialist reference files.

## Seven Key Acts Every Tenant Should Know

### 1. Housing Act 1988

The **Housing Act 1988** is the foundational statute for private renting in England. It created the Assured Tenancy and Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST) frameworks that govern most private tenancies today.

**Key provisions:**

- **Section 5:** Statutory periodic tenancies arise automatically when a fixed-term AST expires.
- **Section 8:** Grounds for possession (landlord seeking eviction on specific grounds, Schedule 2).
- **Section 21:** No-fault eviction (to be abolished by the RRA 2025).
- **Sections 27-28:** Damages for unlawful eviction (calculated as the difference in property value with and without a sitting tenant).
- **Section 22:** Information about assured tenancies to be provided to tenants.

### 2. Housing Act 2004

The **Housing Act 2004** introduced deposit protection and the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS).

**Key provisions:**

- **Sections 213-215:** Mandatory deposit protection in a government-approved scheme within 30 days; prescribed information to tenants; penalties for non-compliance (1-3x deposit).
- **Part 1 (ss.1-54):** HHSRS — risk-based assessment of housing conditions; Category 1 (duty to act) and Category 2 (power to act) hazards.
- **Part 2 (ss.55-78):** Licensing of Houses in Multiple Occupation (HMOs).
- **Part 3 (ss.79-100):** Selective licensing of other residential accommodation.
- **Sections 73-74:** Rent Repayment Orders.

### 3. Landlord & Tenant Act 1985

The **Landlord & Tenant Act 1985** imposes repairing obligations on landlords.

**Key provisions:**

- **Section 11:** Implied covenant to repair structure, exterior, and installations for water, gas, electricity, sanitation, heating, and hot water. Applies to tenancies of less than 7 years.
- **Section 12:** Landlord cannot contract out of s.11 obligations.
- **Sections 9A-9C (inserted by the 2018 Act):** Fitness for human habitation.
- **Section 10:** Factors determining fitness for habitation.

### 4. Protection from Eviction Act 1977

The **Protection from Eviction Act 1977** protects tenants from illegal eviction and harassment.

**Key provisions:**

- **Section 1(2):** Criminal offence to unlawfully deprive a residential occupier of their occupation.
- **Section 1(3):** Criminal offence of harassment — acts intended to cause the occupier to give up occupation.
- **Section 1(3A):** Harassment — acts the perpetrator knows or should know are likely to cause the occupier to leave.
- **Section 2:** Restriction on re-entry without due process of law.
- **Section 3:** Requirement for a court order before evicting most residential occupiers.
- **Section 5:** Validity requirements for notices to quit.

### 5. Tenant Fees Act 2019

The **Tenant Fees Act 2019** bans most letting fees charged to tenants.

**Key provisions:**

- **Section 1:** Prohibition of fees not listed in Schedule 1.
- **Schedule 1:** Seven permitted payments only (rent, deposit capped at 5 weeks, holding deposit capped at 1 week, tenant-requested changes capped at £50, early termination, utilities, limited default fees).
- **Section 8:** Section 21 notices invalid while prohibited payments remain unreturned.
- **Penalties:** Up to £5,000 first offence; up to £30,000 or criminal prosecution for repeat offences.

### 6. Deregulation Act 2015

The **Deregulation Act 2015** introduced retaliatory eviction protection and Section 21 procedural requirements.

**Key provisions:**

- **Section 33 (inserting ss.33A-33C into Housing Act 1988):** Retaliatory eviction — Section 21 is invalid if served within 6 months of the council serving an improvement notice following a tenant's complaint about disrepair.
- **Sections 34-36:** Additional requirements for valid Section 21 notices:
  - Gas Safety Certificate must have been provided.
  - EPC must have been provided.
  - "How to Rent" guide must have been provided.
  - Prescribed Form 6A must be used.
  - Deposit must be protected and prescribed information given.

### 7. Renters' Rights Act 2025

The **Renters' Rights Act 2025** is the most significant reform in a generation. (Not yet in force — see rra2025_timeline.md for implementation phases.)

**Key provisions (when commenced):**

- **Section 1:** Abolition of Section 21 (no-fault eviction).
- **Sections 2-4:** All tenancies become periodic; tenants give 2 months' notice to leave.
- **Sections 5-7:** New and revised eviction grounds (1, 1A, 6A, 8A) with 12-month protected period.
- **Sections 8-9:** Rent increase reforms — Section 13 only, 2-month notice, Tribunal cannot set rent higher.
- **Sections 13-14:** Pet request rights.
- **Sections 28-35:** Private Rented Sector Ombudsman.
- **Sections 36-42:** Property Portal.
- **Section 57:** Anti-discrimination (benefits).
- **Section 58:** Bidding wars ban.

## Landlord Obligations Summary

### Before the Tenancy Starts

The landlord must:

- Carry out a **right-to-rent check** (Immigration Act 2014) — verifying the tenant's immigration status.
- Provide a valid **Energy Performance Certificate (EPC)** — minimum rating of E (or exemption).
- Provide a valid **Gas Safety Certificate (CP12)** — less than 12 months old.
- Provide an **Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR)** — less than 5 years old.
- Provide the government's **"How to Rent" guide** (current version).
- Protect the deposit in a government-approved scheme within **30 days** and serve prescribed information.
- Ensure the property is **fit for human habitation** (Landlord & Tenant Act 1985, s.9A).
- Ensure the property has **working smoke alarms** on every floor and **carbon monoxide alarms** in rooms with solid fuel appliances (Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm (England) Regulations 2015, amended 2022 to require CO alarms in rooms with any fixed combustion appliance).
- Comply with the **Tenant Fees Act 2019** — no prohibited fees.

### During the Tenancy

The landlord must:

- Keep the **structure, exterior, and installations** in repair (Landlord & Tenant Act 1985, s.11).
- Maintain the property in a condition **fit for human habitation** (s.9A).
- Carry out annual **gas safety checks** and provide the certificate.
- Respond to repair requests within a **reasonable time**.
- Give at least **24 hours' written notice** before entering the property (for inspections/repairs) and enter at reasonable times.
- Not **harass** the tenant or interfere with their quiet enjoyment.
- Comply with **fire safety regulations** (particularly in HMOs).
- Ensure **smoke alarms and CO alarms** are in working order at the start of each tenancy.

### At the End of the Tenancy

The landlord must:

- Follow the correct legal process for **ending the tenancy** (Section 21 or Section 8, with valid notices).
- Return the **deposit** (or the undisputed portion) promptly.
- Use **ADR or court proceedings** to resolve deposit disputes — not unilateral deductions.
- Not **illegally evict** the tenant or change locks without a court order.

## Tenant Obligations Summary

### General Obligations

The tenant must:

- **Pay rent** on time as agreed in the tenancy agreement.
- **Look after the property** — keep it reasonably clean and not cause damage beyond fair wear and tear.
- **Report disrepair** to the landlord promptly (in writing).
- **Allow access** for repairs and inspections with proper notice (24 hours' written notice, reasonable times).
- **Not sublet** without the landlord's written permission (unless the tenancy agreement permits it).
- **Not use the property for illegal purposes.**
- **Not cause nuisance or annoyance** to neighbours.
- **Comply with the terms** of the tenancy agreement (unless a term is void or unfair).
- **Give proper notice** to end the tenancy (typically 1 month for a monthly periodic tenancy; 2 months under RRA 2025 when in force).

### What Tenants Are NOT Responsible For

- **Structural repairs** — these are the landlord's responsibility under s.11.
- **Fair wear and tear** — normal deterioration from everyday use.
- **Pre-existing defects** — problems that existed before the tenancy began.
- **Repairs caused by the landlord's failure to maintain** — e.g., damp caused by a roof leak the landlord failed to fix.

## Houses in Multiple Occupation (HMOs)

### What Is an HMO?

A House in Multiple Occupation (HMO) is defined in the Housing Act 2004, s.254. In simple terms, a property is an HMO if:

- It is occupied by **3 or more people** forming **2 or more separate households**, and
- They share one or more basic amenities (bathroom, toilet, kitchen), or the property lacks such amenities.

### Mandatory HMO Licensing

Under the Housing Act 2004 (as amended by the Licensing of Houses in Multiple Occupation (Mandatory Conditions of Licences) (England) Regulations 2018), an HMO must be licensed if:

- It has **5 or more occupants** forming **2 or more separate households** (regardless of the number of storeys).

### Licence Conditions

An HMO licence typically requires:

- A named licence holder (usually the landlord or manager) who is a "fit and proper person."
- Maximum number of occupants specified.
- Fire safety measures (smoke alarms, fire doors, fire blankets, extinguishers).
- Adequate kitchen and bathroom facilities for the number of occupants.
- Adequate waste disposal arrangements.
- An annual gas safety check.
- A valid EICR.
- Compliance with the Amenities and Space Standards.

### Room Size Minimums in Licensed HMOs

The Licensing of Houses in Multiple Occupation (Mandatory Conditions of Licences) (England) Regulations 2018 set minimum room sizes:

- **Single occupant:** 6.51 sq metres.
- **Two occupants:** 10.22 sq metres.
- **Child under 10:** 4.64 sq metres.

### Penalties for Unlicensed HMOs

- **Criminal offence** — unlimited fine on conviction.
- **Civil penalty** — up to £30,000 (as an alternative to prosecution).
- **Rent Repayment Order** — the tenant can claim up to 12 months' rent from the landlord.
- **Section 21 restriction** — a landlord cannot serve a valid Section 21 notice on an unlicensed HMO.

### Additional and Selective Licensing

Local councils can introduce:

- **Additional HMO licensing** — extending licensing to smaller HMOs (e.g., 3+ occupants from 2+ households) in designated areas.
- **Selective licensing** — requiring a licence for all private rented properties in a designated area, regardless of HMO status.

## Subletting

### The General Rule

Most AST agreements **prohibit subletting** or require the landlord's written consent. The tenant must check their tenancy agreement.

### Subletting Without Permission

If the tenant sublets without the landlord's consent (where the tenancy agreement prohibits it):

- The tenant is in **breach of the tenancy agreement** (Section 8, Ground 12).
- The subtenant may have limited rights (an AST subtenancy is valid between the tenant and subtenant, but the head landlord is not bound by it).
- The landlord can seek possession against the tenant.

### Subletting with Permission

If the landlord grants written permission to sublet:

- The tenant becomes the subtenant's landlord and must comply with all landlord obligations (deposit protection, s.11 repairs, etc.).
- The head tenancy and subtenancy are separate.

### Assignment

Assigning the tenancy (transferring it to another person entirely) is different from subletting. Most ASTs prohibit assignment. An assignment without consent is a breach of the tenancy agreement.

## Gas Safety Certificates

### Legal Requirement

Under the Gas Safety (Installation and Use) Regulations 1998, reg. 36:

- The landlord must have all **gas appliances, fittings, and flues** in the property checked by a **Gas Safe registered engineer** every **12 months**.
- The gas safety check must be carried out before the tenant occupies the property (for new tenancies) and then annually.

### The Gas Safety Certificate (CP12)

The gas safety certificate (also known as a CP12 or Landlord Gas Safety Record) must record:

- The date of the inspection.
- The address of the property.
- Details of each appliance checked.
- Any defects found and remedial action taken.
- The engineer's Gas Safe registration number.
- A pass or fail for each appliance.

### Providing the Certificate to Tenants

- **New tenants:** The certificate must be provided **before the tenant moves in**.
- **Existing tenants:** A copy must be provided within **28 days** of the annual check.
- A copy must be provided to any prospective tenant within **28 days** of their request.

### Consequences of Non-Compliance

- **Criminal offence** — prosecution by the Health and Safety Executive (HSE), with unlimited fines and up to 6 months' imprisonment.
- **Section 21 restriction** — a Section 21 notice is invalid if the landlord has not provided a valid gas safety certificate.
- **Civil liability** — if a tenant is harmed by a gas leak or CO poisoning due to an unchecked appliance, the landlord faces substantial damages claims.

## Electrical Safety (EICR)

### Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020

- All private rented properties must have an **Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR)** carried out by a qualified and competent person.
- The EICR must be carried out at least every **5 years**.
- The report must be provided to **new tenants before they occupy** and to **existing tenants within 28 days** of the inspection.
- If the EICR identifies defects requiring urgent attention (Code 1 or Code 2), the landlord must carry out remedial works within **28 days** (or the timescale specified in the report if shorter).
- The council can impose a civil penalty of up to **£30,000** for non-compliance.

## Energy Performance Certificates (EPC)

### The Requirement

Under the Energy Performance of Buildings (England and Wales) Regulations 2012:

- The landlord must provide a valid **EPC** to the tenant before the tenancy begins.
- An EPC is valid for **10 years**.

### Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES)

Under the Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) (England and Wales) Regulations 2015:

- Since **1 April 2018 (new tenancies)** and **1 April 2020 (existing tenancies)**, it is unlawful to let a property with an **EPC rating below E** (unless a valid exemption is registered).
- **Penalties for non-compliance:**
  - Renting a property below the minimum standard: up to **£5,000** per property.
  - Providing false or misleading information on an exemption register: up to **£1,000**.

### Proposed Future Changes

The government has previously consulted on raising the minimum EPC rating to **C** by 2028 for new tenancies and 2030 for existing tenancies. The exact timeline and requirements may change following further consultation.

### Exemptions

Landlords can register exemptions if:

- All cost-effective improvements have been made but the rating remains below E.
- The improvements would cost more than £3,500 (the spending cap).
- The improvements would reduce the property's value by more than 5%.
- Third-party consent (e.g., from the freeholder) has been refused.

## Right to Rent Checks

### Immigration Act 2014

Under the Immigration Act 2014, ss.20-37, landlords must check that prospective tenants have the **right to rent** in England. This applies to anyone aged 18 or over who will use the property as their only or main home.

### What the Landlord Must Check

The landlord must verify the tenant's identity and immigration status by checking one of the following:

- A **British or Irish passport** (or national identity card for Irish citizens).
- A **biometric residence permit** or **biometric residence card**.
- **Share code** from the Home Office online right-to-rent checking service.
- Other acceptable documents listed in the Home Office guidance.

### Timing

- The check must be carried out **before the tenancy begins**.
- If the tenant has time-limited permission to be in the UK, **follow-up checks** must be carried out before the permission expires.

### Non-Discrimination

The right-to-rent check must be carried out for **all** prospective tenants, regardless of their appearance, accent, or nationality. Checking only people who "look foreign" would constitute unlawful race discrimination.

### Penalties for Non-Compliance

- **Civil penalty:** Up to **£3,000 per tenant** for a first breach; up to **£10,000** for repeat breaches.
- **Criminal offence:** If the landlord knows or has reasonable cause to believe the occupier does not have the right to rent, they commit an offence under s.33A (inserted by the Immigration Act 2016), punishable by up to **5 years' imprisonment**.

## Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarms

### Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarm (England) Regulations 2015 (Amended 2022)

- **Smoke alarms:** Must be installed on **every storey** of the property where there is a room used as living accommodation.
- **Carbon monoxide alarms:** Must be installed in any room containing a **fixed combustion appliance** (gas boilers, gas fires, wood-burning stoves, oil-fired boilers). The 2022 amendment extended this from solid fuel appliances only to all fixed combustion appliances.
- The alarms must be in working order at the **start of each new tenancy**.
- **Penalty for non-compliance:** Up to **£5,000** civil penalty.

## Unfair Terms — Consumer Rights Act 2015

### Application to Tenancy Agreements

Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 (Part 2), terms in a tenancy agreement may be **unfair** and therefore **unenforceable** if they cause a significant imbalance in the parties' rights and obligations to the detriment of the tenant. Examples of potentially unfair terms:

- Clauses requiring professional cleaning on exit regardless of the property's condition.
- Excessive penalty clauses for early termination.
- Clauses requiring the tenant to use a specific (expensive) contractor for minor maintenance.
- Clauses waiving the tenant's statutory rights (void under s.12 Landlord & Tenant Act 1985).
- One-sided break clauses that only benefit the landlord.

The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) has published guidance on unfair terms in tenancy agreements.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- Know your rights under the seven key Acts — they protect you from unfair treatment, unsafe conditions, excessive fees, and illegal eviction.
- Your landlord must provide gas safety certificates (annually), EICRs (every 5 years), a valid EPC (minimum E rating), smoke alarms, CO alarms, and the "How to Rent" guide.
- HMOs with 5+ occupants must be licensed — check if your property needs a licence and whether it has one.
- Do not sublet without written permission from your landlord.
- Right-to-rent checks are mandatory but must be applied equally to all applicants.
- Unfair terms in your tenancy agreement may be unenforceable — seek advice if a clause seems unreasonable.
- The RRA 2025 will bring major reforms when commenced, but existing protections continue to apply fully in the meantime.
