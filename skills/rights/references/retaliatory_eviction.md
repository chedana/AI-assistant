# Retaliatory Eviction

## Overview

Retaliatory eviction occurs when a landlord attempts to evict a tenant because the tenant has complained about the condition of the property. In England, the Deregulation Act 2015 (section 33) provides statutory protection against this practice by making Section 21 notices invalid in certain circumstances after a tenant has raised a legitimate complaint about disrepair. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 will strengthen these protections further.

## The Problem of Retaliatory Eviction

### Why It Matters

Before the Deregulation Act 2015, tenants who complained about disrepair risked being served with a Section 21 (no-fault) eviction notice. This created a chilling effect: tenants would live in substandard conditions rather than risk losing their home. The fear of eviction was — and to some extent remains — one of the biggest barriers to tenants exercising their rights.

### How It Typically Happens

1. Tenant reports a problem (damp, broken boiler, pest infestation) to the landlord.
2. Landlord fails to carry out repairs or responds dismissively.
3. Tenant complains to the local council's Environmental Health team.
4. The council inspects and serves an improvement notice on the landlord.
5. The landlord, angered by the council involvement, serves a Section 21 notice to evict the tenant.

## Deregulation Act 2015, Section 33

### The Protection

Section 33 of the Deregulation Act 2015 (which inserted sections 33A-33C into the Housing Act 1988) provides that a Section 21 notice is **invalid** if all of the following conditions are met:

### Condition 1: Tenant Complained in Writing

The tenant must have made a **written complaint** to the landlord about the condition of the property. The complaint must relate to a matter that the landlord is responsible for under their repairing obligations (e.g., s.11 Landlord & Tenant Act 1985).

### Condition 2: Landlord Failed to Respond Adequately

The landlord must have either:

- **Not responded** to the complaint within **14 days**, or
- **Responded** but with an inadequate response that did not address the issue (e.g., a dismissive reply or a refusal to carry out repairs).

### Condition 3: Tenant Complained to the Council

After the landlord's failure to respond adequately, the tenant must have **complained to the local council** about the same issue.

### Condition 4: Council Served a Relevant Notice

The council must have served one of the following on the landlord:

- An **improvement notice** under s.11 or s.12 of the Housing Act 2004 (requiring the landlord to remedy a Category 1 or Category 2 hazard).
- A **notice of emergency remedial action** under s.40 of the Housing Act 2004.

### Condition 5: Section 21 Served After the Council Notice

The landlord must have served the Section 21 notice **after** the council served the improvement notice or notice of emergency remedial action.

### The Effect

If all five conditions are met, the Section 21 notice is **invalid** and the court **must** refuse to make a possession order based on it. The landlord cannot evict the tenant using Section 21 while the conditions remain in effect.

## The Six-Month Protection Window

### Timing

The protection applies where the Section 21 notice is served **within 6 months** of the council serving the improvement notice. After 6 months, the landlord can serve a new Section 21 notice (provided they have complied with the improvement notice and the conditions no longer apply).

### Renewal of Protection

If the council serves a further improvement notice (for the same or different issues), a new 6-month window begins from the date of the new notice. The protection can therefore continue indefinitely if the landlord persistently fails to maintain the property.

## Limitations and Exceptions

### Tenant Caused the Problem

The protection does **not** apply if the condition of the property that led to the complaint was caused by the tenant's own actions or default. For example, if the tenant damaged the boiler and then complained about having no heating, the retaliatory eviction protection would not apply.

### Condition Existed Before the Tenancy

The protection does not apply if the hazard existed at the time the tenancy was granted and the tenant was made aware of it.

### Already in Breach

The protection does not prevent a Section 8 eviction if the tenant is in breach of the tenancy (e.g., rent arrears under Ground 8 or 10). The protection is specific to Section 21 notices.

### Only Applies to ASTs

The Deregulation Act 2015 protection only applies to **Assured Shorthold Tenancies**. However, most private tenancies in England are ASTs.

### Selective Licensing Areas

The protection applies regardless of whether the property is in a selective licensing area, but the existence of licensing may provide additional enforcement tools.

## Interaction with Other Tenant Protections

### Unfair Practices — Consumer Rights Act 2015

Even outside the specific conditions of the Deregulation Act 2015, a retaliatory eviction may be challengeable as an unfair practice. If the landlord's motive for serving a Section 21 notice is purely retaliatory, the tenant can raise this as a defence in possession proceedings, though success is less certain outside the statutory framework.

### Human Rights Act 1998

Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (right to respect for private and family life and the home) may provide an additional defence in possession proceedings, particularly where the eviction is disproportionate. Courts are required to consider proportionality when making possession orders.

### Equality Act 2010

If the retaliatory eviction is connected to a protected characteristic (e.g., the tenant complained about disability-related adaptations), additional protections under the Equality Act 2010 may apply.

## Interaction with the Renters' Rights Act 2025

### Section 21 Abolition

The RRA 2025 will **abolish Section 21** entirely (not yet in force — expected Phase 1 around May 2026 for new tenancies). Once Section 21 is abolished, retaliatory eviction via the no-fault route becomes impossible by definition.

### Strengthened Protections

Under the RRA 2025:

- All evictions will require **specific grounds** (Section 8 grounds, including new ones).
- If a landlord uses a Section 8 ground shortly after a tenant has complained about disrepair, the tenant may argue that the ground is being used pretextually.
- The **Private Rented Sector Ombudsman** (established by the RRA 2025, not yet in force) will provide an additional avenue for complaints about retaliatory behaviour.
- The **First-tier Tribunal** will be able to consider whether eviction proceedings were motivated by the tenant exercising their rights.

### Transitional Period

Until the RRA 2025 provisions are commenced, the Deregulation Act 2015 protections remain fully in force and are the primary defence against retaliatory eviction.

## Practical Steps for Tenants

### Before Complaining

1. **Document the problem** — take dated photographs and videos of the disrepair.
2. **Report to the landlord in writing** — email is best (creates a timestamp). Describe the problem clearly and ask for it to be resolved within a reasonable timescale.
3. **Keep copies** of all correspondence.

### If the Landlord Fails to Respond

1. **Wait 14 days** for the landlord to respond adequately.
2. If no adequate response, **contact the local council's Environmental Health team** and request an inspection.
3. **Confirm your complaint to the council in writing** (email is fine) so there is a record.

### If the Council Serves an Improvement Notice

1. **Keep a copy** of the improvement notice (ask the council for one).
2. If the landlord serves a Section 21 notice after the improvement notice, **do not panic** — the Section 21 is likely invalid.
3. **Seek legal advice** from Shelter (0808 800 4444), Citizens Advice, or a housing solicitor.
4. **Do not leave** the property voluntarily — the landlord must go to court, and you can raise the retaliatory eviction defence.

### If You Receive a Section 21 After Complaining

1. **Check the timeline** — was it served after you complained to the council?
2. **Check whether the council served an improvement notice** — contact the council to confirm.
3. If the conditions of the Deregulation Act 2015 are met, **inform the court** when the landlord applies for possession. The court must refuse the possession order.
4. **Get legal advice** — legal aid may be available for housing possession cases.

## Evidence Gathering

### What to Keep

- All written complaints to the landlord (emails, letters, texts).
- The landlord's responses (or lack thereof).
- Council correspondence (complaint references, inspection reports, improvement notices).
- Dated photos and videos of the disrepair.
- Medical evidence if the disrepair has affected your health (e.g., GP records relating to damp-related respiratory problems).
- Witness statements from other occupants or visitors.

### Timeline Is Key

The retaliatory eviction protection depends on the **sequence of events**: complaint to landlord → inadequate response → complaint to council → council serves notice → landlord serves Section 21. Establish this timeline clearly with dated documents.

## Council's Role

### Duty to Investigate

Local councils have a duty to investigate complaints about housing conditions. Under Part 1 of the Housing Act 2004, councils must take enforcement action where Category 1 hazards are identified.

### Reluctance to Act

Some councils are under-resourced and may be slow to inspect or reluctant to serve improvement notices. If the council fails to act:

- **Escalate** the complaint through the council's complaints procedure.
- **Contact your local councillor** or MP for assistance.
- **Complain to the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman** if the council has failed in its duty.
- **Consider a private HHSRS assessment** by a qualified surveyor and present it to the council.

## Case Law and Precedents

### Issa v Hackney LBC [1997] 1 WLR 956

This case established that councils have a duty to enforce housing standards and cannot simply ignore complaints. Where a council fails to inspect or take action, the tenant may seek judicial review of the council's decision.

### Implications for Retaliatory Eviction

While the Deregulation Act 2015 provides the primary statutory protection, case law supports the principle that tenants should not be penalised for exercising their legal rights. Courts have shown willingness to scrutinise landlord motives when the timing of an eviction notice closely follows a complaint.

## Landlord Penalties for Non-Compliance

### Financial Consequences

If a landlord serves a Section 21 notice that is found to be invalid due to the retaliatory eviction provisions:

- The landlord **cannot recover possession** through this route.
- The landlord may be ordered to pay the **tenant's legal costs** if the tenant had to defend possession proceedings.
- The landlord may face a **civil penalty** from the council for failing to comply with an improvement notice (up to £30,000 under the Housing and Planning Act 2016, s.126).
- The landlord may be subject to a **prosecution** for non-compliance with the improvement notice.

### Rent Repayment Orders

If the landlord fails to comply with an improvement notice, the tenant can apply for a **Rent Repayment Order** under the Housing Act 2004 (as amended by the Housing and Planning Act 2016), recovering up to 12 months' rent.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- You have a statutory right to complain about disrepair without fear of eviction.
- If you complain to the council and they serve an improvement notice, any Section 21 notice served by the landlord within 6 months is invalid.
- Always complain in writing first, then to the council if the landlord does not act within 14 days.
- Do not leave the property voluntarily if you receive a Section 21 after complaining — seek legal advice.
- The Renters' Rights Act 2025 will eventually abolish Section 21 entirely, eliminating the primary retaliatory eviction tool.
- Document everything and keep a clear timeline of events.
