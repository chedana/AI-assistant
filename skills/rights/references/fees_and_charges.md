# Fees and Charges — Tenant Fees Act 2019

## Overview

The Tenant Fees Act 2019 (TFA 2019) dramatically reformed the fees landlords and letting agents can charge tenants in England. It came into force on 1 June 2019 for new tenancies and was extended to all existing tenancies from 1 June 2020. The Act bans most letting fees and caps deposits, providing tenants with clear protection against exploitative charges.

## Scope of the Act

### Who Is Covered

The Tenant Fees Act 2019 applies to:

- **Assured Shorthold Tenancies (ASTs)** in England.
- **Licences to occupy** housing in England (e.g., lodger agreements in some cases).
- Payments to **landlords** and **letting agents** (and any person acting on their behalf).

### Who Is NOT Covered

- Tenancies that are not ASTs (e.g., assured tenancies, regulated tenancies, tenancies with annual rent over £100,000).
- Social housing tenancies (council and housing association).
- Tenancies in Wales (which has its own Renting Homes (Fees etc.) (Wales) Act 2019).
- Commercial tenancies.

## The Seven Permitted Payments

Schedule 1 of the Tenant Fees Act 2019 lists the **only** payments that a landlord or letting agent can require from a tenant. Everything else is a **prohibited payment**.

### 1. Rent

- The **rent** itself is a permitted payment.
- Rent must be a **fixed amount** and cannot vary during a payment period.
- Rent cannot be front-loaded (e.g., charging a higher rent for the first month to disguise a prohibited fee).
- If rent for any period exceeds the rent for any other equivalent period, the excess is a **prohibited payment** unless there is a genuine reason for the variation.

### 2. Tenancy Deposit

- A refundable tenancy deposit is permitted.
- **Cap:** No more than **5 weeks' rent** where the annual rent is less than £50,000.
- **Cap:** No more than **6 weeks' rent** where the annual rent is £50,000 or more.
- The deposit must be protected in a government-approved scheme within 30 days (Housing Act 2004, s.213).
- Any deposit exceeding the cap is a prohibited payment. The excess must be refunded to the tenant.

### 3. Holding Deposit

- A refundable holding deposit to reserve the property is permitted.
- **Cap:** No more than **1 week's rent**.
- One week's rent is calculated as: (monthly rent x 12) / 52.
- The holding deposit must be returned within **15 days** of receiving it if:
  - The tenancy is granted (it is offset against the rent or deposit).
  - The landlord decides not to proceed.
  - The deadline passes without agreement.
- The landlord may **retain** the holding deposit if:
  - The tenant provides false or misleading information.
  - The tenant fails a right-to-rent check.
  - The tenant pulls out of the tenancy without reasonable cause.

### 4. Payments for Tenant-Requested Changes

- A payment for a variation, assignment, or novation of the tenancy **requested by the tenant** is permitted.
- **Cap:** No more than **£50** (including VAT) unless the landlord can demonstrate that greater costs were reasonably incurred.
- Examples: changing the tenancy end date, adding or removing a tenant, permission for a pet (but see RRA 2025 pet rules).

### 5. Early Termination of the Tenancy

- If the tenant wishes to end the tenancy early (before the fixed term expires or before the notice period runs out), the landlord may charge a payment.
- The payment must not exceed the **financial loss** suffered by the landlord. This is typically:
  - Rent due for the remainder of the notice period or fixed term, minus any rent received from a replacement tenant.
  - Reasonable costs of re-letting (but not agent fees if those would be prohibited under the Act).
- The landlord must **mitigate their loss** (i.e., make reasonable efforts to find a new tenant).

### 6. Utilities, Council Tax, and Communication Services

- Payments for **utilities** (gas, electricity, water), **council tax**, and **communication services** (TV licence, broadband, telephone) are permitted.
- These are only permitted where the tenancy agreement requires the tenant to pay them.
- The payment must be to the **supplier** (or reimbursement of the landlord's actual cost). The landlord cannot mark up utility costs.

### 7. Default Fees

- A payment in respect of a **default** by the tenant is permitted in limited circumstances.
- **Late rent payment:** The landlord can charge interest on overdue rent at no more than **3% above the Bank of England base rate** per annum. This can only be charged from the date the rent was due until the date it is paid. No flat fee for late payment is permitted.
- **Lost key replacement:** If the tenant loses a key (or other security device), the landlord can charge the **reasonable cost** of replacement, including the cost of a locksmith if necessary. The landlord must provide written evidence of the cost.
- **Cap:** No default fee may exceed **£50** unless the landlord can demonstrate that the actual costs were higher. If claiming more than £50, the landlord must provide written evidence.

## All Other Fees Are BANNED

### Prohibited Payments — s.1 and s.2

Any payment not listed in Schedule 1 is a **prohibited payment**. This includes (but is not limited to):

- **Referencing fees** — the cost of tenant referencing and credit checks.
- **Administration fees** — charges for preparing tenancy agreements, inventories, or other paperwork.
- **Check-in and check-out fees** — charges for inventory inspections.
- **Viewing fees** — charges for viewing the property.
- **Guarantor fees** — charges for referencing or processing a guarantor.
- **Renewal fees** — charges for renewing or extending a tenancy agreement.
- **Professional cleaning fees** — mandatory pre-move-out professional cleaning charges (though reasonable cleaning costs can still be deducted from the deposit).
- **Credit check fees** — the landlord/agent bears this cost.
- **"Move-in" or "move-out" fees** — any charge for the process of moving in or out.
- **Insurance fees** — requiring the tenant to purchase specific insurance policies (except as part of a pet agreement under RRA 2025).
- **Garden maintenance fees** — charges for a gardener (though the tenant can be required to maintain the garden as a tenancy term).
- **Any fee described as "non-refundable"** — if it is not one of the seven permitted payments, it is banned regardless of what it is called.

### Third-Party Requirements

The ban extends to payments required by the landlord or agent to any third party. For example, requiring the tenant to use a specific (and expensive) referencing company and pay their fee is prohibited.

## Enforcement

### Trading Standards

The Tenant Fees Act 2019 is primarily enforced by local authority **Trading Standards** teams (or the lead enforcement authority). Tenants can report prohibited payments to their local council.

### Financial Penalties

| Offence | First Offence | Repeat Offence (within 5 years) |
|---------|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Requiring a prohibited payment | Civil penalty up to **£5,000** | Criminal offence — unlimited fine or civil penalty up to **£30,000** |
| Requiring a holding deposit in excess of 1 week's rent | Civil penalty up to **£5,000** | Criminal offence or civil penalty up to **£30,000** |
| Failing to return a holding deposit | Civil penalty up to **£5,000** | Criminal offence or civil penalty up to **£30,000** |

### Banning Orders

Under the Housing and Planning Act 2016, a landlord who commits a banning order offence (which includes repeat breaches of the TFA 2019) can be subject to a **banning order** — preventing them from letting property or engaging in letting agency work for a specified period.

### Listed on Rogue Landlord Database

Landlords who receive a banning order or civil penalty are entered on the national **Database of Rogue Landlords and Property Agents** (maintained by local councils). The Renters' Rights Act 2025 will replace this with a publicly accessible **Property Portal**.

## Tenant Remedies

### Recovering Prohibited Payments

If a tenant has paid a prohibited fee, they can recover it by:

1. **Writing to the landlord/agent** requesting a refund, citing the Tenant Fees Act 2019.
2. If the landlord/agent refuses, applying to the **First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber)** for an order requiring repayment.
3. The Tribunal can order the landlord/agent to repay the prohibited payment in full.

### Section 21 Restriction

Under s.8 of the Tenant Fees Act 2019, a landlord **cannot serve a valid Section 21 notice** while a prohibited payment remains unreturned. This is a powerful incentive for compliance.

### Reporting to Trading Standards

Tenants can report breaches to their local council's Trading Standards team. The council can investigate and impose financial penalties.

### Right to Offset Against Rent

If the landlord has required a prohibited payment and the tenant has paid it, the tenant may be able to offset the amount against future rent. However, this should be done with legal advice, as unilateral rent deductions can be risky.

## Holding Deposits — Detailed Rules

### The 15-Day Deadline

The landlord must deal with the holding deposit within **15 days** of receiving it (or a longer period if agreed in writing by both parties — called the "deadline for agreement"). By the deadline, one of the following must happen:

- The tenancy is entered into and the holding deposit is applied toward the first rent payment or tenancy deposit.
- The holding deposit is returned to the tenant.

### When the Landlord Can Retain the Holding Deposit

The landlord can retain the holding deposit only if:

1. The **tenant** has decided not to proceed with the tenancy (without reasonable cause).
2. The tenant provided **false or misleading information** that materially affected the landlord's decision.
3. The tenant **fails a right-to-rent check** under the Immigration Act 2014.

Even in these cases, the landlord must notify the tenant in writing within 7 days of the deadline, explaining why the holding deposit is being retained.

### Multiple Holding Deposits

A landlord or agent cannot take holding deposits from multiple prospective tenants for the same property at the same time. They must return the holding deposit to one applicant before accepting one from another.

## Common Disputes and Scenarios

### Agent Says "The Landlord Requires Professional Cleaning"

If the tenancy agreement requires professional cleaning on exit, this clause may be **unfair** under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 (as it imposes a disproportionate obligation). In any case, the cost of professional cleaning cannot be charged upfront — it can only be deducted from the deposit if the property is genuinely left dirtier than at the start.

### Agent Charges for Referencing

This is a **prohibited payment**. The landlord or agent bears the cost of referencing. Report it to Trading Standards.

### Landlord Charges a Renewal Fee

This is a **prohibited payment**. Renewing or extending a tenancy cannot be charged. The maximum charge for any tenant-requested change is £50.

### Landlord Requires Specific Insurance

Requiring the tenant to purchase specific insurance (e.g., contents insurance from a named provider) is generally a prohibited payment. However, the landlord can recommend insurance and the tenant is free to choose their own provider.

## Interaction with Renters' Rights Act 2025

### Pet Insurance

Under the RRA 2025 (not yet in force), landlords can require tenants to obtain **pet insurance** as a condition of granting consent for a pet. This will be a permitted payment when the RRA 2025 provisions are commenced.

### Property Portal

The RRA 2025 will establish a **Property Portal** where all landlords must register. The portal will include compliance information, making it easier to identify landlords who charge prohibited fees.

## Key Takeaways for Tenants

- Virtually all letting fees are banned. The only permitted payments are rent, deposit (capped at 5 weeks), holding deposit (capped at 1 week), tenant-requested changes (capped at £50), early termination costs, utilities/council tax, and limited default fees.
- If you have been charged a prohibited fee, you can recover it through the First-tier Tribunal.
- A landlord cannot serve a valid Section 21 notice while a prohibited payment remains unreturned.
- Late rent interest is capped at 3% above the Bank of England base rate — no flat fees for late payment.
- Lost key replacement must be at actual cost, with evidence provided.
- Report breaches to your local council's Trading Standards team.
- Keep receipts and written records of all payments to your landlord or agent.
